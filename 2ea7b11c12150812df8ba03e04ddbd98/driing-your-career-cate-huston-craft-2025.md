# DRIing Your Career - Cate Huston (Talk Outline)

> **Cate Huston** (leads client engineering at **DuckDuckGo**; author of *Engineering Leader*; coach; advisor at Twill) on owning your career — golden nugget: **"no one cares more about your career than you — take ownership."** Framing metaphor: tech workers were **pampered Instagram raccoons** (Melanie on a trike, Piper who paints, Tamer with a crypto scam); in this market we're now **Toronto raccoons** — thriving in adverse conditions by being **strategic, determined, and resilient** ("neuron density of a cat, brain the size of a dog").

---

## 1. Own It (be the DRI)

- Poll: many have had a **good manager** (who gave feedback, support, opportunities) — treasure them, write them a note. But you **can't rely on always having one** (busy/distracted/inexperienced), and your career **transcends any single manager**.
- **Expect more from your career and less from your current job** — the thing to optimize is the **overall arc**, not the current job. Sometimes jobs you dislike develop your skills; sometimes jobs you enjoy don't develop you — making the trade-off **explicit** improves decisions.
- **Professional-development plans** made at review time and forgotten are useless — define your **proximate objectives**, next achievable goals, and the career/life you want.

## 2. Proximate Objectives & Planting Opportunities

- A good **proximate objective**: **challenges you but feels possible**, you **learn** something, you **want** to do it, and it's **within your control**. "Get promoted" fails — it's something **someone else gives you** ("promotions are a level of capitalism, and capitalism is a con"); "a good goal is one where **even if you fail, you learn**."
- **Opportunities are like flowers** — sometimes given, but if you want them later you must **plant** now (want to give talks → submit CFPs; want to manage → take on onboarding/docs; want staff → go toward hard problems). Her book (2023 offer, out last year) grew from years of writing/talks building a profile ("being a person who could write a book was also really hard").
- **Be discerning** about given opportunities (a rose vs. an unwanted tulip vs. a dandelion "for someone else") — when opportunity feels scarce we accept everything, so learn to **create your own**, doing things you actually enjoy. When someone asks you for something, it's a **great time to negotiate** — she reframed "token woman" speaking invites as **"negotiation practice."**
- **A job title is a few words; the work is 40+ hours/week.** Don't cling to a title at the expense of the work you value (a staff engineer on her team "fixes a terrible problem while also shipping" — not everyone wants that). People who fought hard for titles hold them tightly — valid, but don't sacrifice the work and growth. Account for your **life-moment** (up for a challenge? need stability? need rest?), write down **constraints**, and discuss them with your manager ("the worst they'll say is 'not possible,' and often much more is possible than you think").

## 3. Strategy, Dreams & the Raccoon Crew

- **Career dreams** = far-out someday/maybe (concrete or directional); **proximate objectives** = the next steps; **strategy** = how you connect them (identify gaps, generate ideas). You don't need a **5-year plan** — just enough **direction** to make the next decision. You may be in **execution** mode or an **exploratory** phase (she's resting/exploring after her book).
- **Write down the plan:** the **what** (specific enough to know you've achieved it, broad enough that reaching it expands your capability), the **why** (your underlying motivation, to look at when discouraged), the **resources**, and **who** will support you.
- Build a **support system ("raccoon crew")** — you can't get all needs from one person (true in romance too): manager, **skip-level** (broader reach/info for opportunities), **peers** (context, feedback, moving things along), **mentors/sponsors** (advocate when you're not in the room), **coaches** (invested in you without org/social baggage), and the **work BFF** (data says life is better with one). "Don't be alone in Toronto."

## 4. Becoming Coachable

- **Feedback is just information** — sometimes usable to improve, sometimes it tells you about the giver, sometimes it goes in the trash. Growth feels hard ("**drowning rats / boiling frogs**" — both capture "can I do this / sustain this?"). Even good managers aren't good for everyone all the time (their own constraints), so few people get exactly what they need when they need it.
- The sweet spot is **out of your comfort zone with help** — find the *right* help. Distinguish **mentoring / managing / coaching** (they're not interchangeable; **Marcus Buckingham's *First, Break All the Rules***: develop **strengths**, fix only a **controlling weakness**). Mentoring that teaches you to "swim in a pool" is useless if you're "drowning in the ocean."
- **Coachability = receptiveness × actionability.** Highly receptive people work through feedback, are self-aware, fit it into (and adjust) their mental model, and believe they can learn from anyone; highly actionable people **experiment/iterate** and respond to **implicit** as well as explicit feedback. Be in **different quadrants with different people** (high-trust up-right, low-trust down-left) — healthy and understandable.
- **Five-step plan to be more coachable:** (1) build **self-awareness** (when someone annoys you, what did they trigger/teach?); (2) get a **broad perspective** (tech isn't that special — learn from other industries); (3) **shed defensiveness** (get curious); (4) **admit when you screwed up** (start with what could go better so people can add to it); (5) **ask for advice** (a "magic trick" — people prefer giving advice to feedback).
- **Know when to bin feedback:** she ignores **unsolicited criticism from men on the internet** for her mental health; but years of feedback about her "annoying voice/accent" eventually yielded a real truth — her **pacing is too fast** (still working on it). **Bad feedback** ignores broader context (misses misogyny), **negates your input** (a boss who won't say what he wants then complains you didn't guess), or **undermines rather than helps you succeed** (Netflix's *No Rules Rules* direct feedback works *because* it's meant to help you succeed — you can take much harder feedback that way). Feedback meant to **tear you down** tells you about the giver — bin it, or learn to work around/avoid them. Untangling old **feedback trauma** is a gift to your resilience.

## 5. Knowing When to Quit

- She's become the "**quit your job coach**" — but there's a big difference between quitting because you **can't get what you want** and quitting **from power** knowing what you want and going toward it. Think about quitting **annually** (she's at her 5-year anniversary because the org changed and she got better at constructively reconsidering).
- **Signals to pay attention to:**
  - **Learning** — the saddest resume is "5 years of experience = 1 year, five times." If you're not learning (esp. when you should be), change it (maybe by leaving, maybe not).
  - **Coping mechanisms** — learning political dances or blame-proof documentation is energy that won't help (and may harm) you in a better environment.
  - **Morally conflicted about hiring** — if you think others deserve better, maybe you do too.
  - **Confidence** — when you feel yourself getting *smaller* not bigger, it's corrosive (how abusive environments keep people).
  - **Physical** — "your job won't love you back"; take care of yourself.
  - **Market reality** — in a hot ("Toronto") market, some frustrations are industry-wide, so **interview regularly** to know how hard it'd be to find another job and whether it'd actually be **better** ("if you find out you're not underleveled/underpaid, use that to be less annoyed — 'I have a good deal under capitalism'").
- **Takeaways:** get clear on **what you want** (question what capitalism tells you); understand your **terrain/constraints**; have a **strategy** (deliberate learning + iterating on proximate objectives, not a 5-year plan); **build your raccoon crew**; invest in your own **learning/development** and your relationship with feedback.

## 6. Q&A

- **"Morally conflicted about hiring"?** It can mean unfair hiring practices, or just: *would you recommend this org to a friend?* If no, ask why.
- **Work vs. title when the title pays more?** The million-dollar question — money and title aren't the same either; decide how much you care about **money on what time frame** (her partner contracts between jobs — most money ever, but not a great long-term career move, so fine for a year, not forever). Also, orgs have real constraints (no headcount to promote).
- **How to encourage feedback?** **Ask for advice**; and going up the org chart, mine **implicit** feedback (nobody has full context on your job — she has 9 reports and a boss who by design doesn't know her details).
- **Keeping patience while waiting for the right opportunity?** Be clear about what you're getting now — "if it's just a paycheck to buy time, that's fine, and being clear helps you be less frustrated."
- **Low confidence + unsupportive manager — where's the motivation?** Fix **confidence** first — collect small **wins** (in corrosive grad school she started a weekly blog to "be in the world" and got engagement her advisor never gave).
