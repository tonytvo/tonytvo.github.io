# The real secret to high-performing teams - Joseph Pelrine (Talk Outline)

> Joseph Pelrine (psychologist, agile pioneer; trained as a hospital emergency responder) on **Crew Resource Management (CRM)** — the real secret to high-performing teams, from NASA/aviation/medicine.

---

## 1. The Problem: Non-Technical Skills

- Talented individuals often "act like a bunch of idiots" as a team. Like software, teams need functional *and* non-functional requirements — technical skills make a high-performing *individual*; **non-technical skills** make a high-performing *team*. HR hires for technical skills and doesn't set up the environment. Three psychological error areas: **human factors** (cognitive overload — the live **Stroop test** induced mistakes by clashing word/color/language; mistakes rise from every ~30 min at rest to every ~30 sec under stress), **fixation errors** (cognitive tunneling — Google found devs debug the *wrong thing* ~50% of the time; the invisible-gorilla/triangle illusions), and **communication errors**.

## 2. Crew Resource Management

- A **set of ground rules** for action and interaction so a team can apply its technical skills as well as possible "when the hits the fan" — from NASA → aviation → medicine → many fields. Turns the usual model around: **psychological safety is the *result*, not the prerequisite.** Via Kurt Lewin (behavior = f(person, environment)) and social complexity: **culture is a derivative** (you can't change it directly — it emerges from micro-level actions/interactions). Ground behaviors that build **trust** → culture of **psychological safety**. (XP is itself CRM — ground rules for *how a team interacts*; one great XP team turned the practices "up to 11" *under stress* because trusting everyone's quality freed their attention.)
- Lufthansa mixes crews so no one has flown together, then makes them a high-performing team in a **15-minute pre-flight briefing** — impossible without CRM. The anesthesiologist's line: "I need everyone to speak up... how else can I see my blind spot?" (the *first* person he asks at a rescue is the **ambulance driver** — can we get back?). Based on McGrath & Larson's four things a team does (bring in ideas, situational awareness, decide between alternatives, divide/execute work), with ~15 practices (know your environment, communicate clearly, cross/double-check, call for help early, re-evaluate, etc.).

## 3. Systemic Barriers Over Blame

- **Person-centered** view (people are bad/sloppy → fear/punishment) vs. **systems** view (human error is a *consequence*, not a cause). **Swiss cheese model** (Reason): errors become incidents only when moving holes in systemic barriers line up. Don't ask "how can we get better?" — ask "**how can we make it systematically harder for errors to become incidents?**" (Finland painting reindeer antlers reflective; a pharmacy's barcode/double-check chain adding ~1 minute).
- **Fixation:** the **"10-for-10"** — stop for 10 seconds, breathe, discuss the next 10 minutes with the team (Lufthansa's **FOR-DEC**: Facts, Options, Risks/benefits, Decision, Execution, Check — "Scrum on medicine"). **Communication:** closed-loop protocols (medicine's **SBAR**, aviation challenge-response, kitchen "yes chef"). **70% of production problems come from human factors, not tech errors** — CRM is for performance, not just crisis.

## 4. Getting Started & Q&A

- Download the checklist, pick **one** habit, try it, review in retrospectives ("when something happens, blame the systems' defenses, not the person"). Q&A: a manager's most important skill is **trust + role-modeling** (be a leader, not a manager); dislikes military metaphors; let the **team decide** the rules (Kent Beck's "let's *try* this" with a **sunset date**); introduce rules gradually.
