# Connect Business Goals and Your Daily Work with Impact Mapping - Büsra Coskuner (Talk Outline)

> Büsra Coskuner (product coach) on "**tame your product manager**" — using **impact mapping** to connect business goals to daily work so engineers and PMs stop fighting over "the right thing to build."

---

## 1. Stan, the Triangle, and the Trees

- **Stan** (your PM) says the prioritization tool spat out the next feature; you argue for fixing the buggy search, page speed, and tech debt ("won't that make customers happier?") — Stan says **No**. The problem isn't *having different ideas* (you want many, from different minds) — it's **fixating on "the right thing" while misaligned on what you're trying to achieve** ("make customers happy" is too vague).
- **The triangle (trio/triad):** designer + engineer + PM on eye level, same information, deciding together based on evidence — with a **shared language, shared ownership, and shared understanding** of "driving business with products customers love" (ignoring business → run out of cash; ignoring customers → unsustainable).

## 2. Impact Mapping (Gojko Adzic, 2012)

- A tree with five levels connecting **business goal → daily work**: **Goal** (SMART, ≤3–6 months, a metric), **Actors** (who can help/hinder — segmentation, find the biggest lever), **Impact** (the **behavior change** you want — start/stop/do-differently/do-more-often), **Deliverable** (any solution — feature, process, copy change), and the fifth level. **Three twists:** (1) product-management wording — *outcome* not impact, *solution/output* not deliverable ("never say project manager to your PM"); (2) use the last level to **decide what to do next** (enough evidence → deliver; not enough → experiment); (3) her favorite — use it to structure **product discovery** (blend with opportunity-solution trees). Your **zone of control** shifts from just solution/delivery to the **outcome space** once you're in a trio.
- Examples (Airbnb-style "increase first-time US bookings 10%"): pick the biggest-lever actor → the behavior change ("understand which region fits their needs, faster") → and the *engineers'* solutions ("improve region-search relevance via metadata filtering", "auto-detect magic setup") that a PM would never propose — *that's your zone of control*. Real-world maps are **messy, and that's okay**.

## 3. Three Decision Questions (framework-agnostic)

- **What are the biggest levers?** (narrow down at each level — actor, then behavior, then solution). **Is it a known, a belief, or an assumption?** (known = fact/proven; belief = strong evidence, too costly to prove more; assumption = one weak data point) — make thoughts **explicit** ("our biggest customers said they'd buy it" → actually *one*, no commitment = an assumption); you can consciously proceed on an assumption *and then experiment*. **Where is the win-win?** (between what the business wants and what customers want).

## 4. Reverse Impact Mapping & More

- **Reverse impact mapping:** marketing wanted "an internal SEO-metadata tool" (~4 months → a "project" → 9–12 month queue); working *backwards* to their real goal (increase organic traffic) let them offer a **2-hour CSV upload** instead — they took it. Also use impact maps to capture a feature's current state, **rationalize a backlog** (what to say no to), or scope a new product. The **taming**: you and your PM both want to build the right thing — get **specific about the business goal and customer outcome**, find the win-win, align, and even a "disagree-and-commit" sticks. Upgrade your own **product thinking** to understand their world.
