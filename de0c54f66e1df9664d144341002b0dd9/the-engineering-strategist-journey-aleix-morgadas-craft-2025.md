# The engineering strategist journey - Aleix Morgadas (Talk Outline)

> Aleix Morgadas (engineering-strategy consultant) shares his own overwhelming journey into strategy — to help others avoid a couple of the mistakes he made.

---

## 1. Two Failed Modes

- **Execute fast (2014):** more lines of code = ship faster = more money — "going fast to nowhere," burnout, blaming others, unable to learn from his own mistakes. **Copy others (2016):** add whatever's hyped (today: AI agents, platform engineering, Team Topologies colors, layoffs). Delegating strategy to an LLM produced a "reasonable"-sounding B2B marketplace plan costing $8.5–25M over 5 years — copying still reproduces the same bad outcome because *you're not making the decisions*.

## 2. Understand the Business First

- **Follow the money** — talk to whoever controls it. B2B/B2G/B2C, bootstrapped vs. VC, services (scale by people) vs. SaaS (scale by machines), **unit economics** (LTV, CAC), sales-cycle length/churn all shape what strategy works. Understanding the platform/break-even business model finally explained *why companies lay off* (reach break-even faster → raise more from VCs). **Maps analogy:** you don't know exactly where you're going (a direction/area, not a point), and you can't overstep — go step by step with experiments.

## 3. What (Engineering) Strategy Is

- Inspired by Wardley + Snowden (2018). *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* (Rumelt): goals/inspirational words/fluff aren't strategy — **negate it**: if the negation is absurd, it wasn't a real decision. A strategy = **decisions under imperfect data to overcome a high-stakes challenge**, leaving you better positioned. **Engineering strategy** is the same, from engineering's view, and must be *coherent* with the business one.
- Strategy is a **cycle** (go through it faster than competitors to learn faster), a **fractal** (nested strategies at different scopes/time-horizons that must be coherent, not competing), and both **deliberate** (written, aligning) *and* **emergent** (how well-trained the org is to respond). Design *and* execution must involve the same people — handing over design to executors is lose-lose. Components (Rumelt): **purpose/context → diagnosis → direction (guiding policy) → clear actions**; all collaborative; "don't simplify what isn't simplifiable"; the direction is a **hypothesis** with reviewable objectives (not a vision quote); actions must be scoped enough to track and verify the diagnosis. Use the Cynefin frame — clear = just a pipeline (no strategy needed), chaotic = escape it first; strategy shines in the **complex/non-linear** space.

## 4. A Concrete Story

- Hired as EM (3 months) to help a "customer-focused" company finish and ship. **Diagnosis:** tech is always messy (blaming it is easy), but the real signal was **human** — handovers between back-end and front-end teams, no full-stack teams, leaders leaving every ~6 months, "feature-factory" teams. Kanban maturity: he *thought* they were "customer-driven," but day-to-day was overburden/heroics/unpredictable. **Direction (emergent):** start a **new full-stack team** with chosen practices, from a changed **hiring process**, isolated "in a cave" from leadership pressure, trained, incrementally replacing accidental complexity (Ktor→Spring Boot in a month, zero downtime). Result: a 5-person full-stack team delivering fast, earning trust (backed by a supportive VP) — but some specialists left ("I only want to do JSON APIs"), taking undocumented knowledge (non-linear consequences to live with). Saying **no** to "here's 20 developers" is hard (unused resources become your fault) — 2022's job market helped him be bold.

## 5. Culture, Context & Sharing

- Finishing a strategy positions you in a **new context *and* a new culture** ("how things get done here" — Tania Reilly's dimensions). You **can't change culture so your strategy works** (by the time you did, it's outdated) — instead, **success reshapes culture** (what works becomes "how we do things," which later becomes past inertia). His model: **culture (what's possible) + business *challenge* (context) + current context (what's feasible) → strategy (hypothesis) → outcome reshapes culture/context**. Call to action: **share engineering strategies** like we share open source (his `awesome-engineering-strategy` repo). Q&A: bottom-up momentum (find 1–2 excited people per team; ~1.5 years to build); avoid getting fired by mapping — he made it **collaborative co-creation**, asked genuine questions rather than challenging the narrative, and had a protective VP.
