Listening Over Speaking - June 4, 2026 General notes

Overview • Conference Driven Management (CDM) and Psychological Safety Misuse: Speaker B describes a pattern where managers adopt buzzwords like ‘psychological safety’ from conferences without proper understanding, leading to employee malicious compliance — doing exactly what is told with visible resentment and nothing more. The real barrier to psychological safety is not fear of speaking up, but the inability to listen, driven by emotional triggers. • Three Types of Triggers Blocking Effective Communication: In professional settings, triggers cluster into: (1) Truth triggers — defensive reactions to feedback regardless of accuracy; (2) Relationship triggers — reactive to who is delivering the message (‘who are you to say this to me?’); (3) Identity/Ego triggers — perceiving requests as threats to one’s competence or identity. Triggers fire within 40 milliseconds, with the amygdala hijack fully engaging the fight/flight/feed/mate response at ~400 milliseconds. • Crew Resource Management (CRM) as the Real Foundation of Team Safety: CRM, originally developed by NASA after the 1977 Tenerife air disaster (583 deaths), was adopted in aviation and medicine to reduce errors through structured communication protocols. Unlike psychological safety frameworks, CRM builds an environment where speaking up is expected and legitimate, making psychological safety an outcome rather than a prerequisite. Key protocols include: SBAR (patient handover), I’M SAFE (pre-shift personal readiness check), ISBAR, Four-D (decision-making), and closed-loop communication. • Practical Techniques for Managing Triggers and Improving Communication: Speaker B recommends: keeping an anger diary to identify trigger patterns; asking ‘where are you open to criticism?’ and ‘where are you available to be seduced?’; physiological regulation through breathing techniques (box breathing 4-4-4-4; 4-1-6-1; or 4-7-8 method); using structured communication protocols adapted from emergency medicine for software team standups and handovers. Checklists are described as ‘training wheels’ — tools to internalize communication habits, not permanent fixtures. • Real-World Application — Ferrari Pit Stop Model Applied to Hospital Patient Handovers: Great Ormond Street Hospital (London) experienced critical risks during neonatal cardiac surgery patient transfers to the ICU. They collaborated with a Formula 1 team (Ferrari, Maranello) to optimize handover protocols, using the pit stop model. The resulting structured handover system became the basis for the UK-wide SIPS protocol for multi-patient shift handovers, combining ISBAR, Four-D prioritization, and closed-loop communication.

Topics Introduction: Conference-Driven Management (CDM) and the Problem with Psychological Safety • Speaker B opens with an anecdote: a venture capital firm asked him to assess a struggling portfolio company; upon entering, he immediately identified the issue • Management had fallen into a pattern he calls ‘Conference Driven Management (CDM)’: attending conferences, adopting the latest idea (e.g., psychological safety), then switching to the next trend weeks later with no sustained implementation • The company had heard about psychological safety, brought in a business trainer for a workshop, but the constant talking without action drove employees into ‘malicious compliance’ — doing exactly what is told and nothing more, with visible resentment • Eric Hofer quote cited: ‘Every great cause begins as a movement, becomes a business, and eventually degenerates into a racket’ — applied to the psychological safety industry

The Real Problem: People Don’t Know How to Listen • Speaker B reframes the psychological safety problem: the issue is not that people are afraid to speak up — it is that people do not know how to listen • This reframing positions listening and trigger management as the core intervention needed

Interactive Exercise: Honest Self-Assessment of Listening Ability • Speaker B asks the audience to honestly answer: ‘Are you someone people feel comfortable talking to?’ and share the answer with the person next to them • Follow-up challenge: did participants answer honestly, or did they rationalize and justify to themselves or their neighbor? • Purpose: to demonstrate that self-assessment around listening is itself subject to bias and defensiveness

The Neuroscience of Triggers and the Amygdala Hijack • A trigger is a stimulus (smell, sound, something said) that produces a sudden, involuntary reaction rooted in past experience, even when the current situation is different • Triggers go directly to the amygdala, bypassing rational thought; the amygdala sends signals to the hypothalamus, setting off a physical stress response • This is Daniel Goleman’s ‘amygdala hijack’: the rational brain goes offline while the threat response takes over • Timeline: stimulus hits amygdala → first reaction at 40 milliseconds (Paul Ekman’s facial micro-expression window); amygdala processes at ~125ms; P300 ERP event-related potential (visible on EEG) fires at ~300ms; hypothalamus triggers fight/flight/feed/mate response at ~400ms (under half a second)

Three Types of Professional Triggers with Examples • Truth Triggers: Immediate reaction that what is being said is wrong, regardless of accuracy. Example: a developer receiving a messy code review reacts defensively before evaluating whether the feedback is valid. Example: a new team member suggesting a different daily standup format is heard as ‘you’re doing this wrong’ by long-tenured team members • Relationship Triggers: Reaction to who is saying something (‘who are you to say this to me?’). Example: a new team member’s suggestion triggers authority challenges from experienced team members • Identity/Ego Triggers: The message feels like a verdict on one’s competence or identity. Example: a senior engineer asked to pair with a junior on a ‘straightforward task’ perceives it as punishment or an insult to their expertise — nothing about competence was said, but the identity trigger fires • Speaker B notes it would be surprising if anyone in the room had not experienced at least one of these triggers, either as the triggered person or as the one accidentally triggering another

Examples of Trigger Response Patterns in Public Figures • Speaker B illustrates trigger response patterns using public examples mapped to the three trigger types • Truth trigger response: ‘That’s a stupid question. That’s fake news.’ • Relationship trigger response: ‘Who are you? Your ratings are terrible. Nobody watches you.’ • Ego/Identity trigger response: Escalating self-aggrandizement — ‘No one has ever done what I’ve done,’ ‘I’m the healthiest president in history,’ citing unnamed people (‘Sir, you’re the greatest in history’) and making vague threats • Additional example from Air Force One: accusation of treason against a reporter for incorrect reporting; excessive use of ‘I’ rather than ‘we’; use of the word ‘treasonous’ and calls for punishment • A contrasting lighter example: when asked which James Bond movie he’d like to be in, a similar dismissive, triggered response occurred before the subject walked away • These examples demonstrate how trigger responses follow predictable, observable patterns across all three categories

Root Cause Summary: Triggers Prevent Listening and Psychological Safety • Speaker B concludes the diagnostic section: people are triggered too easily and lack the capacity to listen effectively • The correct starting point for building psychological safety is working on trigger awareness and management, not top-down safety mandates

Practical Techniques: Recognizing and Managing Your Own Triggers • Step 1: Learn to recognize your own triggers — identify the stimuli that produce reflex reactions, many of which occur before conscious awareness (within 40ms) • Tool: Keep an anger diary — whenever angry, pause and write down the specific stimulus; over time, patterns of personal triggers become visible • In emotional intelligence work, anger is treated as data, not a problem to manage. Anger is a secondary emotion sitting on top of something deeper: hurt, fear, or a sense of disrespect • Two key reflective questions from psychotherapist Beth Fuchs: (1) ‘Where are you open to criticism?’ and (2) ‘Where are you available to be seduced?’ — both positive and negative stimuli can trigger amygdala hijack • Example of positive trigger/seduction: a manager tells a senior developer they’re ‘the only one who understands the legacy codebase’ and asks them to come in on the weekend — flattery and indispensability are triggers • Triggers include: flattery, recognition, and the feeling of being the indispensable expert

Physiological Techniques to Interrupt Trigger Responses • Most impactful technique: physiological breathing intervention, achievable in under 60 seconds • Technique: breathe in deeply, when lungs feel full pull in one more breath, then exhale quickly — produces a calming (‘chill’) effect • Also useful for sleep difficulties: repeat a few times over a couple of minutes • Box breathing: inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4 • 5-7 technique (Speaker B’s variant): inhale 4, hold 1, exhale 6, hold 1 (described as 5-7) • 4-7-8 technique: inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8 • All techniques are doable in under 60 seconds

Amy Edmondson’s Psychological Safety Research — Reexamination • Amy Edmondson coined ‘team psychological safety’ in 1999; studied surgical and postoperative work teams • Finding: the best-performing teams were not those that made the fewest mistakes, but those that talked about and worked through their mistakes • Speaker B’s critique: Edmondson is a genius but is a business professor, not a surgeon or psychologist; she observed something she had no firsthand expertise in and filtered it through a business lens • Her conclusion — that psychological safety is the prerequisite for team function — is challenged; Speaker B argues the best teams were actually doing something different that made psychological safety an outcome, not a precondition

Crew Resource Management (CRM): Origin, Design, and Principles • CRM was originally developed by NASA following the 1977 Tenerife Airport disaster, where two planes collided on the tarmac killing approximately 500 people (Speaker B says 583) • NASA’s study led to a set of communication and coordination techniques rapidly adopted in aviation, then in medicine • CRM purpose: reduce errors by reshaping culture through communication norms, authority gradients, and coordination under pressure • CRM trains people to: brief explicitly, challenge respectfully, share situational awareness, cross-check decisions, and speak up • CRM does not ask people to ‘feel safe and hopefully speak up’ — it builds a structured environment where speaking up is expected and legitimate • Psychological safety is the outcome, not the precondition • Speaker B learned CRM working at a small hospital in a village of ~200 people in the Swiss Alps, where everyone was trained as a first responder, on call within 10 minutes for avalanche and ski injury emergencies • CRM focuses on four areas: situational awareness, teamwork, leadership, decision-making and task management — all grounded in communication

Dr. Gamma Polo and the Principle of Actively Seeking All Voices • Dr. Gamma Polo: former head of an emergency department in Berlin, now head of anesthesiology and emergency medicine; has conducted over 9,000 ambulance rescues and over 3,000 helicopter rescues, including in war zones • Key teaching from Dr. Polo: ‘I demand to hear everybody’s voice because I don’t know where my own blind spot is’ — a demonstration of low ego and high situational humility • Example: on an emergency ambulance call, the first person Dr. Polo asks is the driver — to confirm whether the road back to hospital is accessible, whether the hospital is admitting patients, whether a helicopter is needed • Principle: every voice on the team has potentially critical situational information

CRM Communication Protocol 1: COSTA (Escalating Concern in the OR) • Protocol for a scrub nurse (or anyone lower in hierarchy) to raise a concern to a head surgeon • Step 1: ‘I am concerned about this’ • Step 2 (if no reaction): ‘I am uncomfortable because I notice [specific symptom/observation]’ • Step 3 (if still no reaction): Declare it a safety issue — procedure stops • Provides a clear, legitimate, escalating protocol that removes the social risk of speaking up

CRM Communication Protocol 2: I’M SAFE Pre-Shift Checklist • Used by emergency rescue teams before each shift; each team member self-assesses • I — Illness: any illness affecting performance? • M — Medication: any medication that could impair performance? • S — Stress: under significant stress? • A — Alcohol: any alcohol that could affect performance? • F — Fatigue: am I tired? • E — Emotion: any emotional issues? • Final question: ‘Can you count on me?’ • Speaker B notes he has also used this protocol with software development teams

CRM Communication Protocol 3: ISBAR for Patient/Situation Handovers • Structured information transfer protocol commonly seen in hospital shows • I — Identity: patient name (if known and relevant) • S — Situation: vital signs, blood pressure, current status • B — Background: history (e.g., hypertension, recent medication changes) • A — Assessment: clinical interpretation (‘this looks like it may be due to…‘) • R — Recommendation: proposed action (‘I recommend starting X’)

CRM Communication Protocol 4: Four-D (Critical Decision-Making) • Developed by Lufthansa; used in critical decision-making scenarios • Based on the ‘10 for 10’ principle: take 10 seconds (deep breath, pause) to plan the next 10 minutes — counters System 1 (fast, intuitive, potentially wrong) thinking • D1 — Facts: what do we know? • D2 — Options and Opinions: what are the different courses of action? • D3 — Risks and Benefits: evaluate the different paths • D4 — Decide, Execute, Check and Review: assign work, act, and review; loop back when anything changes or at the end of the time box • Speaker B draws a direct parallel to software development: ‘This is the adult’s way of doing the baby’s scrum’

Closed-Loop Communication • Ensures mutual understanding by requiring the receiver to repeat back what was heard and the sender to confirm • Cockpit example: Pilot says ‘Nicole, you have the flight controls’ → Co-pilot says ‘I have the flight controls’ → Pilot confirms ‘Yes, you have the flight controls’ — both parties are certain the message was received and understood correctly • Restaurant kitchen example: Sous chef calls ‘two sirloins, medium rare’ → Grill chef repeats back ‘two sirloins, medium rare, chef’

Great Ormond Street Hospital / Ferrari Pit Stop Collaboration • Great Ormond Street Hospital (London), a premier children’s hospital, was experiencing critical errors during neonatal cardiac surgery patient handovers from the operating room to the ICU • Patient handovers are identified as ‘the biggest risk in medicine’ • The hospital approached a Formula 1 team (Ferrari, Maranello) — a client of Speaker B’s (though he was not directly involved in this project) — known for exceptionally fast, precise handovers (pit stops) • A surgical group traveled to Maranello and was trained by Ferrari; Ferrari engineers then visited the hospital, observed and recorded all procedures, and returned to optimize the patient handover process • The result was a protocol modeled on the Ferrari pit stop: clearly assigned roles, synchronized actions, explicit communication • This approach evolved into the SIPS protocol used across the UK for full shift handovers involving multiple patients • Sickest patient first using SIPS framework • For each patient: ISBAR • Prioritization: Four-D • Verification: Closed-loop communication

Respondent Fatigue and Moving Beyond Checklists • Respondent fatigue: a psychological concept describing loss in data quality when too many questions are asked or the same questions are repeated too frequently • Dr. Polo’s advice (after 30+ years): ‘These checklists are your training wheels — eventually you have to get rid of them and find out what really works for you’ • The goal is not to follow the checklist; the goal is what the checklist builds — internalized communication habits and team culture • Speaker B challenges the audience: how will they change their daily standup away from the formulaic ‘what did I do yesterday / what am I doing today / do I have blockers?’

Q&A: Autistic Communication Styles and Structured Protocols • Audience question: could communicating like autistic people (direct, no social exclamations, literal) make teams more efficient and conflict-free? • Speaker B: interesting question worth thinking about; structured burst communication protocols serve a similar directness function • Book recommendation: The Checklist Manifesto by Atul Gawande — described as the definitive book on how to write and use checklists and how to eventually move past them

Q&A: Working with AI Agent Teams • Audience question: how can CRM/communication techniques bridge gaps when teams work more with AI agents than human colleagues? • Speaker B: honest acknowledgment — he does not currently work with teams using AI agents in practice, only theoretically; uses AI for research • Observation from psycholinguistics: many people are polite to their AI (say ‘please’), anthropomorphize AI (thinking of Claude as ‘he’ or ‘she’), which reveals interesting psychological dynamics • Expressed willingness to work with development teams that include AI agents

Q&A: Opening Dialogue When Your Presence is Already a Trigger • Audience question: what to do when your own presence on a project is already a trigger for a coworker? • Speaker B’s advice: First, honestly ask yourself whether you are the problem (revisiting the earlier self-assessment exercise) • Managers/people in positional power should be cautious about intervening directly — using power (reward, coercive, or legitimate power per French and Raven’s power theory) will likely worsen the situation • Instead, find an intermediary with personal power — either expertise-based (expert power) or relationship-based (referent power) — someone both parties respect • Mandated interventions (e.g., platform or process mandates from leadership) tend to cause equal and opposite resistance reactions • Real-world example: a large Swiss insurance company trained all their Scrum Masters as certified mediators — this became so effective that they were constantly called into other departments across the company to mediate disputes; the company had to hire additional Scrum Masters to keep up with demand • Additional recommendations: mental health first aid training; trained mediator roles within teams
