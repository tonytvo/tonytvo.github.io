# Forest & Desert & Genie – Kent Beck (Talk Outline)

> Kent Beck (at 65, "ambitions far outstripping my capabilities — and suddenly I have new capabilities") on developing software with **"the genie."** A time of enormous change and real fear: "What will our job even be in 2 or 5 years?"

---

## 1. Craft, Economics, and New Capability

- Two of his books (*Smalltalk Best Practice Patterns*, *Implementation Patterns*) describe skills that **lost their leverage** — writing human-readable code is still a pleasure but has *"zero economic value"* now. When **craft and economics misalign, economics wins**.
- He did ~**1,000 hours of augmented development** in a year (vs. ~hundreds in the *previous five*) — because dependency-hell frustration is gone. As you go junior → senior → "super senior," your **world of ideas shrinks** (too much work to finish them); the genie **re-expands** it ("I want to write a parser generator — and now I can").

## 2. Why "Fire All the Programmers" Makes No Sense

- It's happened before (1994: "offshoring + OOP will end programming jobs"). We're now **more valuable and more profitable** — so wanting *fewer* of us doesn't add up → likely **temporary**. His 3am story: **in 5 years there will be more programmers**, after disruption — big employers shed jobs, those people start companies, most fail, some succeed spectacularly and hire everyone back. Meanwhile, be **less annoying**: empathize, speak others' language (finance, kitchen-cleanliness analogies).

## 3. Forest vs. Desert (amplified by the Genie)

- **Desert:** assume **scarcity** → rush, skip tests, let relationships erode → a self-fulfilling prophecy (yet extremely profitable). **Forest:** assume **sufficiency** → help each other, learn, build tools, reflect → self-sustaining (not *safe* — thorns and predators — but enough to go around).
- The **genie amplifies whichever you're in**. Desert + genie → tighter control, "spend more tokens" (measuring diesel instead of loads moved), throw in 25 then 250 agents — none of which fixes scarcity *because the assumption is the problem*. Forest + genie → more time for experimentation, learning, alternatives (he'll work weeks, dump it, start over, and still be more productive). Be **suspicious of the finger guns** ("Great idea, boss!").

## 4. Features vs. Futures

- Every feature you add **burns futures** (optionality) — entropy must be paid. The genie is **laser-focused on features, indifferent to futures**, so teams "bounce along the bottom" (all counters dirty) — now a *single person* reaches that mess in days.
- **The alternative (Pablo Casals: "I rest between the notes"):** after each feature, take the **space between features** to *restore optionality and a bit more* — refactor, rename, ask the genie to explain ("tell me a bedtime story"), explore alternative implementations, add tests/fuzzing/mutation/coverage, ask **"which file is hardest for you to manipulate?"** and split it. Each investment in understanding/structure **creates future options** → bigger things get *easier* over time. For an inherited "100 features / 0 futures" project: throw away if personal; otherwise **add two units of futures per feature** while keeping the feature stream flowing (that's how you build trust).

## 5. Multiplayer Mode & the Emotional Landscape

- A troubling trend: **single-player mode** (shrink teams to one — "the perfect size because you can't disagree with yourself"), which **misses human interaction**, the most valuable thing. Challenge to toolmakers: design **multiplayer from the start** (he did his first genie-**mobbing** in Switzerland; stayed focused the whole time).
- **Nobody knows** whether TDD-with-the-genie works — not "I don't know," *nobody* knows — so **run lots of little experiments in community** (and try the **stupid ideas** if not illegal/immoral/irreversible — no competition there; "my stupidity is a renewable resource"). Everyone is moving through **grief** (denial → bargaining → anger → depression → acceptance) as hard-won skills lose leverage; treat your own and others' reactions with that lens. **Everyone is panicking** — programmers, managers (1 manager / 50 reports), execs (made-up R&D % targets from boards/investors).

## 6. The Playbook Is Wiped Clean

- The pre-genie **playbook** (turn to a page, apply a known fix) is gone — like 1986→2001 OOP → Agile Manifesto, we're back to **writing a new one from shared experiments** (patterns, unit tests, TDD, refactoring came out of that). The **skill of writing a playbook differs from applying one.** Don't rush a manifesto (the Agile one took **15 years** — technology changes faster than humans, so humans change *slower*).
- **You feel behind, but statistically can't all be** — you only see your Dunbar-sized cohort; being *at Craft* puts you on the ahead side. Let "behind" motivate experiments and conversation, not doom-scrolling.
- **Q&A:** CS degrees are "pre-programmer" (like chemistry is pre-med) — need **longitudinal, multi-person projects**, maintaining your own code, tests/refactoring/big-O/correctness, less rote data-structures. On "we don't need marketing/non-programmers": he called out his own younger "jerk" attitude — **it takes a whole team**; he spent 9 months learning to value a business/marketing hire. "We'll accomplish more together than we ever could alone."
