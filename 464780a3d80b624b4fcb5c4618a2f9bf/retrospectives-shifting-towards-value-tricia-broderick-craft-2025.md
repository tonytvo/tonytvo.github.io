# ESSENTIALS - Retrospectives: Shifting Towards Value - Tricia Broderick (Talk Outline)

> Tricia Broderick (co-author of *Lead Without Blame*) on retrospectives that aren't "communicate more / go through the motions." A retro (drawing on Larsen & Derby's *Agile Retrospectives*, now v2 with Horowitz) exists to **reinforce delivering value**, not because "it's the end of the month."

---

## 1. The Five Stages

- **Set the stage:** get a vibe ("which duck are you today?" — honor where people are), **focus the topic** (not "the whole sprint" → generic "communicate more"; pick something specific like "communication with stakeholders"), be explicit about the **goal** (1–2 highlights, 1–2 improvements) and **how the decision will be made** (start with **majority**, not consensus), and **motivate** by framing it as *their* problem (surface the pain), e.g. "share where stakeholder communication caused breakdowns."
- **Gather data (collaborative brainstorm):** a "1-2-4-all"-style flow (think alone → pair to *one* → merge groups) — individual first gives safety, pairing pre-empts **collaboration over competition**, and narrowing shows where the energy is (10 people × 3 ideas is too many). (Facilitation gem: "raise your hand to quiet the room.")
- **Generate insights (the step most teams skip):** promote healthy **conflict** — move sticky notes along an **impact** axis, ask "does anyone disagree?", dig into vague items ("registration"), and rule out **naming people** (the system contributes). Use the "**elephant in the room**" prop, but don't pick too-big a topic (psychological safety) before the team is ready.
- **Make the decision:** easy *if* you did stage three (options narrow to 2–3). Beware **dot-voting** (anchoring bias, wanting to join the winning group); anonymous voting is worse (a team that can only decide by *not* talking isn't a team) — better to **number options** and write your vote first.
- **Close:** focus on **next steps** (who owns each action, by when) and **celebrate/appreciate** the *behavior* (collaboration, conflict, deciding together) so they return — try **"hopes and wishes"** instead of singling people out.

## 2. Bonus Tips

- Pick topics **within the team's control** (a dev team endlessly complaining about marketing → made "marketing copy present" a definition-of-ready → work stopped stalling). Retros need **design/facilitation effort**; **switch up activities** (stop the sailboat); first retro **≤30 minutes**; duration fits the topic (not an arbitrary 3 hours); give people the **right to pass** (a reluctant "John" participated because activities weren't all large-group shout-outs). Go for the **majority tipping point** on value.
- Q&A: for teams that only whine, start from **their** biggest problem; every technique works **remotely** (whiteboard tools, breakout rooms); form action points from the narrowed decision; a persistently disruptive member is usually a **1:1 conversation** (one person had been *weaponized* in past retros — safety must be rebuilt); "can't change it" often still has a slice **in your control** (or get radically transparent about impact).
