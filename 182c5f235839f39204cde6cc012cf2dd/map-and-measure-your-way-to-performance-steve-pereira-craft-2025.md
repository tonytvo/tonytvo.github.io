# Map and Measure your way to Performance - Steve Pereira (Talk Outline)

> Steve Pereira (Visible value stream consulting, co-author of *Flow Engineering*) on mapping and measuring your way to performance — finding the real constraint and getting the *right* things done, not just more things.

---

## 1. Get the Right Things Done

- Flow engineering is about **getting the right things done** — being productive in the *wrong* direction just creates waste and distance from the goal. Organizations chase shiny objects (AI mentioned in "triple digits" today) — noise, not **signal**. The signal is actionable insight that moves the bottom line, and it's usually **low-hanging fruit** in *how* we work, not the latest tool.
- **The $20M story:** a large org wanted help justifying automated artifact deployment. Mapping the value stream showed artifact deployment **wasn't the bottleneck** — two other opportunities had 10× and 100× the impact. The proposed $20M / 18-month investment would have had **zero** effect on lead time. Let the data pick the target.
- **This applies to AI:** if your constraint isn't code creation, adding a code-gen bot just piles work against the existing constraint (breaking one dam into another reservoir with a dam). Optimizing anywhere that isn't the constraint — including downstream of it — won't improve throughput. Address the **choke points** that actually limit what the organization can deliver.

## 2. See the Whole Value Stream

- Delivering value usually means traversing the entire org chart (approvals up/down/across) — a **value stream** that most orgs don't have defined or share a language for. Without end-to-end visibility, teams **micro-optimize** in silos (the same trap as adding AI where you aren't constrained). **Value stream mapping** represents the whole flow and measures it, giving **focal points** so you can prioritize instead of trying to improve everything at once — and it lets **business and tech** argue over the same picture and agree where to focus.

## 3. The Five Mapping Activities

- Start from value and build clarity toward enabling flow — each map builds on the last: **Outcome mapping** (discover pains/goals/context, affinity-map noise into signal, distill a **target outcome** for the next ~3 months, understand why it matters, obstacles, next steps — always set a clear target first); **Value stream mapping** (trace backwards from delivered value; add **cycle time and wait time** — precision matters less than **relative** values, since an activity taking weeks dwarfs ones taking hours; a 2-year, $2M release process revealed easy wins by running steps in parallel, dropping "mandatory" steps, and combining meetings — often just getting people in one room for the first time in 19 years).
- **Dependency mapping** (dig deeper *only where there's gold* — find the vein, don't strip-mine; each workshop targets ~90 minutes), **Future state mapping** (design a better system of work), and the **Flow roadmap** (prioritize by easy × valuable on a now/next/later roadmap — but crucially with **measures of progress, owners, and a clear narrative** for leadership). Surfacing opportunity without acting is worse than nothing.

## 4. Where to Apply It

- Roles: the **value stream** participants, a **key sponsor** (easier to engage with clear artifacts), a neutral **facilitator**, and **representation** across the stream. You have a value stream anywhere value reaches customers (platform delivery, hiring, even event planning) — start where you feel **friction**, where people get **stuck/unclear**, or where **value is missing/misunderstood**. Mapping many streams surfaces **common issues** (fix a shared platform → raise all boats). Prime candidates today: **where to invest AI**, untangling **dependencies** (mitigate, don't just "manage"), aligning business and tech, and questioning rituals like PI planning. Q&A: value stream mapping is a loose, pragmatic subset of **theory of constraints** (aim for an actionable focal point within your sphere of influence, not necessarily the system's ultimate constraint); imperfect/variable data is fine — highlighting variation is itself a valuable signal to drive toward predictability.
