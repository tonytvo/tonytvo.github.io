## 2. AI as Both Risk and Help

### 2.1 AI in recruitment can hurt
- Arina: in the age of AI (used across domains, including recruitment), ask whether AI **boosts diversity or the reverse**.
- Example: **Amazon halted an HR system** because it **downgraded résumés containing the word "women's."**
- Be attentive and think **critically** about these tools.

### 2.2 The EU AI Act
- Zach commends the **European Union's AI Act** for calling out **human-resource use cases as high-risk** and requiring additional scrutiny.
- Don't let AI's promise become "another step back" — keep **humans in the loop** in important processes.

### 2.3 "AI is what you teach it"
- Noémi: AI can pick up **exclusion patterns** — "AI is what you teach it," so it isn't always objective. "Just as much as it could help, it could hurt."

### 2.4 The moderator's two AI concerns
- Gabriella notes AI panels at conferences are often **all-male**.
- And she read that **94% of the administrative workforce is done by women** — the first jobs to go with AI's advance. What can companies do?

## 3. Widening the Pipeline

### 3.1 Broaden who you target
- Noémi (relaying Zach's pre-panel point): if you keep **targeting the same people**, you get the **same numbers** — change the perspective and broaden who and how you target.

### 3.2 Provide learning at work, not just "on your own time"
- Zach: it's easy for an org to just tell employees "learn the skill outside work."
- But candidates from diverse backgrounds often face challenges that don't map onto "traditional white men with spouse/extended-family support for child-rearing and elder care."
- Serious investment means providing **learning experiences at work**, since such candidates may not have extra time outside work.

### 3.3 Lead by example
- Arina: showcase good examples — if you represent a minority group, don't be afraid to **take a stand, mentor, sponsor**.
- "Sometimes we think we're alone… but it isn't like that" — communicate with peers and leadership.
- AI could even analyze **senior leaders' career paths** to find common patterns and experiment with more diverse backgrounds.

### 3.4 Representation lowers barriers
- Noémi: lack of representation makes people see a barrier — reasons not to join, upskill, or learn.

### 3.5 Promote from within
- Zach: hiring senior roles **only externally** means "inheriting every other company's bad decisions" about diversity (interviewing only stereotypical candidates who already had that job).
- Fix: invest heavily in **teaching and learning** so employees can grow and be **promoted from within** — "I don't see another way to fix this."
- Gabriella and Arina: "there should be a funnel."

## 6. Loneliness in Male-Dominated Rooms

### 6.1 Noémi — the observation
- New to tech, initially overwhelmed; a few months in, she looked around and "couldn't name one woman I'd worked with in three months."
- Not discrimination — a very inclusive group — but "it would be nice to have more of us here."
- It's not only about being a woman: a foreigner, a person of color, anyone can feel they "don't have their little minority group to support" them.
- Since then, **Tesco put a lot of effort into hiring women** — now she sees women in the office, "which makes me happy" — the representation feels nice.

### 6.2 Arina — loneliness intensifies at senior levels
- At junior levels there's hope of gender balance; higher up there are fewer minority people, and "**that's the real loneliness**" — competition is higher and stronger, with **no sponsorship**.
- Two-way fix:
  - Share your feelings: "I'm a bit lost, there's a boys' chit-chat I don't understand — help me be part of the team informally, not just formally."
  - Teammates should recognize the pain of that loneliness and be the first to extend a hand: "How do you feel today? Let's have a coffee, let's discuss work issues over coffee." It must be a **dialogue**, not one-way.

### 6.3 Zach — hire senior roles from within (callback)
- Reinforces the pipeline point: only hiring senior people externally inherits other companies' bad diversity decisions.
- Invest in teaching/learning so people can be **promoted from within**.

## 7. Practices to Start Early and Widen the Pool

### 7.1 Tesco's "Girls' Day"
- Noémi: Tesco ran a **Girls' Day** bringing **high-school girls** in to show what tech work is like — broadening horizons **before careers even start**.

### 7.2 Part-time for every role
- Every Tesco position is now offered as **part-time** too — flex time around **life stage**, having children, caring for someone ill, or being ill yourself — widening the hiring pool beyond gender/ethnicity.

### 7.3 Programs emphasizing human connection
- Zach: successful programs focus **less on formal training** and more on **space to ask questions, feel safe, and build connections** that benefit people throughout their time.

### 7.4 Coaching a blended leadership style
- Arina: a vice president who advocates for diversity encourages his pool to be proactive and unafraid, and even teaches female directors **"how to interrupt."**
- Women are often perceived as "nice girls smiling," where interrupting/assertiveness seems bad.
- He inspires a **blended leadership style** — sometimes kind, sometimes pushy — "otherwise you will not reach your goals, and that's okay."

## 8. The KPMG "Hard Work" Finding

### 8.1 The finding
- Gabriella: a newly published **KPMG** study found that for women in the workplace, **"hard work"** has overtaken **leadership/team-management skills** as the most important thing — suggesting it's getting harder for women to thrive.

### 8.2 Noémi — why "hard work"?
- Finds it "really interesting" — what does "just hard work" even mean, and why would it be most important? It signals things are changing and getting **more difficult for women**.

### 8.3 Zach — "hard work" is an unmeasurable, biased metric
- Organizations feel increased pressure to **show profit and deliver value**.
- He's nervous about metrics like "hard work" because it's hard to measure — a manager tends to see "hard workers" as **people who work like me, look like me, speak like me**, unintentionally leaving people out.
- Better: get **concrete and measurable** about performance criteria.

### 8.4 Noémi — merit-based, not KPI-based
- Diversity has become a **metric**, but be careful: you can't **exclude the majority** just to hit metrics.
- Hiring must be **merit-based** — find the genuine good fit; increasing the **pool** lets you find amazing minority candidates.
- Don't "overdo it"; keep standards stable so women aren't criticized "for being a woman at the workplace."

### 8.5 Arina — smart work and unequal home load
- We fulfill many roles (employee/business owner, mom, spouse, friend) — sometimes you should "work **smart**, not hard."
- Strategic KPIs may need a hard push, but that **sacrifices other life spheres** — and women are usually the first to sacrifice, being the first/only to handle kids, household, elder care.
- There's **no equity** in the division of home responsibilities; "it's not about corporate life, it's about the whole life."

### 8.6 Noémi — hire for perspective, not KPIs
- D&I groups can provide support and adjust for minorities' lives.
- "KPIs are not everything" — hire diverse groups **for the perspective**, not to hit diversity KPIs; an all-white-male company has "one restricted perspective" and misses cultures and life experiences.

## 11. Cross-Cultural / Geographically Distributed Teams

### 11.1 Noémi — divided teams need effort
- Her team has **development in Hungary** and **product management in the UK**; it "feels very divided."
- Constant effort is needed to be "a team together," not "the Hungarians and the British people."

### 11.2 Arina — individualist vs. collectivist cultures, ground rules
- International teams may mix **individualist and collectivist** members.
- Speak openly about differences ("we come from different cultures, but we need common ground") and **work out ground rules** that vary team to team.

### 11.3 Zach — dedicated time, never all-but-one co-located
- Make **dedicated time** for cross-geography communication — it builds relationships and trust.
- What "works horribly": everyone in one location **except one person** — "a great way to drive people out of your organization."
- Invest in **understanding cultures**.

### 11.4 The Indian-food anecdote
- Zach's worst example: a British coworker and an Indian coworker had a huge fight because the British coworker insisted **Indian food was a British invention** — "so culturally insensitive." "Don't do that."

### 11.5 Managing conflicts without bad intent
- Zach: when there's no bad intent, **pause and move the conversation up a level** — talk about what was **intended** and understood, not what was literally said. Understanding where the breakdown was (often cultural) lets you navigate around it.
- Noémi: **meeting in person** (even once or twice) really helps build connections.

## 12. Neurodiversity

### 12.1 Noémi — set expectations, discuss accommodations
- Clearly set expectations and communicate as the neurodivergent person; discuss role goals and possible **accommodations** with your manager.

### 12.2 Arina — understand strengths and fit
- Communication is key to accommodate; understand a person's **strengths** and where they'll be the perfect fit.

### 12.3 Zach — educate, then accommodate
- First **understand the whole topic** — run educational sessions on what neurodiversity is, its types, and how to fit people into the workplace.
- Make **accommodations** — e.g., people with **ADHD** dislike noise, so create a **quiet office corner** (like the "silent part of the train") where they can work comfortably.
