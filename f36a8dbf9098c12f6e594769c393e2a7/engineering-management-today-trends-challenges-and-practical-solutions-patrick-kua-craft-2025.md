# Engineering Management Today - Trends, Challenges and Practical Solutions - Patrick Kua (Talk Outline)

> Patrick Kua (coaches EMs and CTOs) on how the forces of the last 25 years — cheap capital, hypergrowth, cloud, big data, LLMs, the pandemic — reshaped engineering management, and four current shifts with practical ways to navigate them.

---

## 1. The Forces That Got Us Here

- "Engineering manager" is a relatively new term (development manager → team lead → EM), with different **archetypes/shapes**. Cheap/zero-interest capital + alternative funding (VC, angels, crowdfunding) + falling cost of building → **hypergrowth** → more teams, bigger teams, more EM positions (people thrown in with little support). The diagram now runs in **reverse**: high interest rates, headcount reductions, fewer management roles.

## 2. Shift 1 — Productivity Pressure ("more with less")

- Everyone's asked to prove productivity though no one agrees what it means. **Ruthlessly prioritize** (Eisenhower matrix; skip dopamine "time fillers"; break big projects into visible milestones; give yourself permission to leave things imperfect). Connect every team's daily work to a **top-level company goal** (autonomy/mastery/purpose — Dan Pink) — critical for platform/infra teams whose customers are other technical teams.
- **Measurement:** DORA four key metrics are *lagging* indicators — the *Accelerate* book's **24 capabilities** are what you actually influence. **SPACE** (Forsgren) says productivity can't be one metric but was hard to apply. The **DX Core 4** (2024, opinionated, practical) recommends per-category metrics: **speed** (diffs per engineer, averaged — never individual), **effectiveness** (developer-experience index, time-to-10th-PR), **quality** (change-failure rate, perceived quality), and **impact** (**% time spent on new capabilities** vs. firefighting legacy). Communicate concretely: "lead time 4 weeks → 1 week," "change-failure 30% → 5%," "planned work 20% → 40%."

## 3. Shift 2 — Role Expectations (the "unicorn EM")

- Leaner orgs → fewer middle managers, EMs managing **larger groups** (12–20+) and expected to be **more hands-on** (technical challenges in hiring; even writing code). Orgs want "someone good at everything." Advice: show **competencies across disciplines** (architecture *and* flow/delivery *and* people); if you know the principles, AI tools are "good autocomplete" to get hands-on again (he writes Python via tools without prior production Python). Early-career EMs get stretched — invest in personal development.

## 4. Shifts 3 & 4 — Hybrid Work and AI

- **Hybrid:** most people dislike fully remote *or* fully office; tension between CEOs/landlords (RTO) and employees (flexibility). Decide a **cadence** for your team, use in-office time **deliberately** (whiteboarding, relationships, sprint planning for alignment; async afterward), and secure **team space**. Expect variation between individuals and coordination friction across teams.
- **AI:** pressure from CEO (more with less), product (integrate it), and skeptical engineers ("this fad will pass" — it won't). Adopt an **experimental mindset**: give teams time/innovation tokens to play, start with **internal/test code** not production, spread knowledge, set a **traffic-light AI policy** (data safety, approved tools). Use AI as **your pair** too (drafts, getting back into coding) — but keep the human review.
