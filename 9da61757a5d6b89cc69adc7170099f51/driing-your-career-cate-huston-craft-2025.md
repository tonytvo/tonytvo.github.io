# DRIing Your Career - Cate Huston (Talk Outline)

> Cate Huston (leads client engineering at DuckDuckGo; author of *Engineering Leader*) on owning your career — "no one cares more about your career than you." The metaphor: tech workers were **pampered Instagram raccoons**; in this market we're now **Toronto raccoons** (thrive in adverse conditions by being strategic, determined, resilient).

---

## 1. Own It (be the DRI)

- Good managers give feedback, support, and opportunities — treasure them (write them a note) — but you can't rely on always having one, and your career **transcends any single manager**. **Expect more from your career and less from your current job**: sometimes jobs you dislike develop you, and jobs you enjoy don't — make the trade-off explicit.

## 2. Proximate Objectives & Planting Opportunities

- A good **proximate objective**: challenges you but feels possible, you learn something, you *want* to do it, and it's **within your control** ("get promoted" fails this — a promotion is something someone else gives you; "a good goal is one where even if you fail, you learn"). 
- **Opportunities are like flowers** — sometimes given, sometimes you pick from what you **planted** earlier (her book came from years of writing/talks). Learn to **create** opportunities doing things you actually enjoy, and be **discerning** (reframe "token woman" invites as *negotiation practice*). A **job title is a few words; the work is 40+ hours** — don't cling to a title at the expense of the work you like; account for what life-moment you're in (challenge vs. stability vs. rest) and discuss constraints with your manager.

## 3. Strategy, Dreams & the Raccoon Crew

- **Career dreams** = the far-out someday; **proximate objectives** = the next steps; **strategy** connects them (you don't need a 5-year plan, just enough direction for the next decision). Write down the **what / why / resources / who**. Build a **support system ("raccoon crew")** — you can't get all needs from one person: manager, **skip-level**, peers, mentors/sponsors, coaches, and the **work BFF** (data says life is better with one).

## 4. Becoming Coachable

- **Feedback is just information** (usable, tells-you-about-the-giver, or trash). Growth feels hard ("drowning rats / boiling frogs"); the sweet spot is **out of your comfort zone with help**. Distinguish mentoring/managing/coaching (Buckingham's *First, Break All the Rules*: develop strengths, fix only *controlling* weaknesses). **Coachability = receptiveness × actionability**; be in different quadrants with different (trust) people.
- **Five-step plan:** build self-awareness (what does that annoying person trigger/teach?), get a broad perspective (tech isn't that special), shed defensiveness, admit when you screwed up (offer a starting point), and **ask for advice** (people prefer giving advice to feedback). Know when to **bin** feedback — bad feedback ignores context, negates your input, or undermines rather than helps you succeed (Netflix's direct feedback works because it's meant to help you succeed); untangling old feedback trauma is a gift to your resilience.

## 5. Knowing When to Quit

- She's become the "quit your job coach" — but there's a difference between quitting because you can't get what you want and quitting **from power** knowing what you want. Think about quitting **annually**. Signals to watch: you've stopped **learning** ("one year of experience five times"); you're building **coping mechanisms** (political dances, blame-proof documentation); you feel **morally conflicted about hiring** friends in; the job shrinks your **confidence**; it affects you **physically** ("your job won't love you back"). In a hot ("Toronto") market, interview regularly to know **how hard it'd be to find another job** and whether it'd actually be **better**. Takeaways: get clear on what you want, understand your terrain/constraints, have a strategy, build your crew, invest in your own learning.
