# Xin Yao – Podcast Stage (Conversation Outline)

> ABK Podcast interviews socio-technical architect **Xin Yao** (Copenhagen; DDD, collaborative modeling, psychological safety) on the real question behind "how to plug AI into your team": it's a human/relational problem, not a tooling one. *(Auto-captions render her name as "Signe.")*

---

## 1. There Is No "Plugin"

- "Plugging AI in" sounds like a clean tooling/process question, but underneath sits **existential threat** — individuals ask "should I jump before I'm pushed?"; teams ask "will it be me?"
- Until this **lack of safety** is addressed (individually and collectively), the brain's higher-order functions stay offline and rational questions (tooling, process, dynamics) can't be answered well.
- We're in **survival mode**; the goal is to move toward a **"thrival"** narrative (survival + thriving, both at once).
- Echoing Kent Beck's keynote: **there is no plugin.** Asking better questions beats having answers — the LLMs have the answers; humans should own the questions.

## 2. Identity in Crisis & the Need to Grieve

- Expert transactional skills (design, modeling, coding, analysis, architecture) are being **commoditized** by the machine.
- Letting go of the identity/skills that brought past success requires a **grieving process** — as a team, not just individually. Without proper letting-go, you can't embrace the new.
- Xin Yao ran a workshop using **event storming + user-needs mapping** to surface the **possibles** (use cases), **lovables** (the deeper need beneath a use case — e.g., not "AI writes my emails" but the real need), and **undiscussables** (what fear keeps us from naming).
- The missing skill is **social/conversational, not technical** — plus trust in ourselves to navigate it.

## 3. Control Was Always an Illusion

- CS attracted many of us *because* it gave control (theory → code → it compiles → you're in charge). LLM non-determinism shatters that paradigm.
- Denial ("I'm still in control, just steering the genie") is one stage of grief.
- But coding was **never fully deterministic** — coffee, sleep, a fight with your spouse all changed your output. We've always dealt with "human slop"; AI is just the **force multiplier**, now producing "AI slop."
- **Harness engineering** is a valid, defensive way to reclaim structural integrity (Fred Brooks' structural integrity: security, reliability, reproducibility) — but a team also needs **conceptual integrity**, which requires daring to **diverge before converging**, not just harnessing.

## 4. Trust Is an Old Problem With Good Practices

- Trust in code you didn't write (or wrote while tired) predates the "genie." Existing practices carry over:
  - **DDD** → ubiquitous language shared between business domain and code (removes the translation loop).
  - **TDD** (Feathers, Beck) → confidence that intended business logic keeps working under change.
  - **Architecture-as-code / ArchUnit** (Neal Ford) → not a harness but a **change sensor**; a broken arch test is good news — change was *detected*.
- These free up frontal-cortex capacity otherwise consumed by fear.

## 5. Agency: Influence the System From the Bottom Up

- **Autopoiesis** (self-creation): every component of a living system can also *influence* that system. You can "hack" a retrospective by starting a conversation, e.g., via **Liberating Structures** — pose the question "how do we feed business context into the LLM? what are our preferences? how do we decide?"
- Don't wait for CTOs/CEOs to mandate; use **bottom-up enabling constraints** (a good question is a constraint that frames attention).
- **Conversational leadership** is now a core differentiator for every engineer: start, facilitate, and summarize (with LLM help) these conversations.

## 6. Reframing the Mandate: Fear ↔ Excitement

- Emotions are symmetrical: fear/anxiety is often **excitement with the sign flipped**. What could we be excited about?
  - **Ideation partnership** with the LLM — "Einstein in the basement" (Henrik Kniberg): your brain extended with the world's biggest wisdom library.
  - Reframing yourself from **code producer → "value engineer"** (Simon Wardley-style): what value am I helping create?
- Rather than confronting your manager, **ask for the outcome**: what changes if I use vs. don't use the LLM? how much freedom do I have to choose the traditional tool where it's better?
- The perennial shift: **from output to outcome**, lead with the *why*, not the *what* (John Cutler). Tech debt now multiplies faster than we catch it — add **"continuous comprehension"** to the CI/CD pipeline.

## 7. What Teams Should Deliberately Invest In

- **Technical:** build your "moat" (harnessing will standardize/productize over time); shift the capacity stack toward **product thinking** — user-needs mapping, defining value/outcomes like a mini-PM.
- **Relational (the real lift):** *transactional* knowledge is commoditized, but **relational knowledge — making knowledge relational — is the differentiator.** Invest in collaborative modeling, Liberating Structures, Peter Block's Six Conversations, "common good protocols."
- **Socio-technical paradigm shift:** the well-being/**thrival of the social system** is as important as the software output — not merely a means to an end.
- **Self-power over "agency":** system thinking means seeing your own power to influence the system (she prefers "empowered" since "agency" now belongs to agents). A soft skill that becomes a **hard skill** with AI in the game.

## 8. Audience Q&A — Team Handed Cursor/Claude

- Don't just impose rules *or* pure chaos: run a **collaborative workshop** to agree the working interface (solo vs. divide-and-conquer) and to ask *why* you're doing this.
- Beware the **first-mover trap**: one person's spec-driven `.md` file "that's fantastic" means nothing to teammates **until you make them relate to it**. The hard, unanswered question: *how do you make them relate?*
