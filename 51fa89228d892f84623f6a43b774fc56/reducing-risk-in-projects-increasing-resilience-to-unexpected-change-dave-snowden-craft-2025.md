# Reducing risk in projects, increasing resilience to unexpected change - Dave Snowden (Talk Outline)

> Dave Snowden (Cynefin creator) on applying **complexity science** — *not* systems thinking — to projects: you can't know in advance what will happen; the only certainty is that whatever you do has **unintended consequences**.

---

## 1. Emergence & What You Can Manage

- A complex/**entangled** system: connections explode combinatorially (10 dots → 3.4 trillion patterns) so "joining the dots" only works in **hindsight**, which doesn't give foresight (the flaw of root-cause/lessons-learned). **Emergence** means you can't deduce the whole from the parts — you **trigger emergence and monitor for patterns**, feeding good ones and starving bad ones; the more you commit to an outcome, the higher the risk (option theory).
- Preconditions: many **actants** (actors, **constraints**, **constructors** — things that transform without changing, e.g. processes, rituals; changing clothes makes coders find more bugs), rich local interaction (a termite nest has no directing intelligence), and *no agent thinking holistically* (a systems-thinking error). So you need **active monitors** and **scaffolding** (rigid constraints for predictability, e.g. driving on the left; Naples "swarming" driving).

## 2. Estuarine Mapping

- A low-cost (two half-days) technique: brainstorm actants, and **if you can't agree what something is, break it down until you do** (no power/authority intrusion). Map onto **energy cost of change × time to change**; draw the **counterfactual line** (won't change — where over-idealistic vision statements land), a **liminal** zone (someone higher could change it), and the **volatile** zone (turns on a dime, high risk). Then day two: **nudge the environment** so the project is more likely to succeed. Watch for **dark actants** (visible effects, unknown cause). Back-of-a-napkin test: a sensemaking framework must be drawable from memory.

## 3. Distributed Decision-Making

- Humans are bad individual decision-makers (the invisible-gorilla experiment: 83% of radiologists miss a gorilla 48× a nodule — "you don't see what you don't expect"). We evolved to compromise in **groups of ~5–7** (bigger groups retreat to silos). Use **role-based groups + the Panopticon effect** (observed by an unknown auditor → honesty): assemble four roles + one anonymous agent, document context/decision/consequences, and they may spend small sums freely → **real resources follow what works, not who advocates loudest** (used in villages/development banks).

## 4. New Portfolio Risk Matrix & Sensing

- Two axes: **natural vs. forced emergence** × **fine vs. coarse granularity** → four risk-mitigation modes (traditional strategy, parallel safe-to-fail probes, opportunistic, and many small stimulated experiments) — the map forms a chaos symbol; map each part of your project into a domain to choose its mitigation. **"People are objects too"** — architect a people-object with standard I/O that also generates **AI training data** ("if you don't know where the training set came from, don't use the AI"; scathing on West-Coast cultural bias → the need for **epistemically balanced training sets**).
- **Walk the gemba / micro-narratives:** replace periodic project reports (edited with hindsight) with continuous **micro-capture** by engineers (100% compliance at West Point) → a real-time **human sensor network** and weak-signal detection (metal fatigue at Boeing "doesn't feel right"; whistleblowing/microaggressions captured anonymously before they're "serious enough to report"). Prefer **multimedia** (text is <10% of what you know). **HeXi**: break every agile method into its lowest coherent unit so you can recombine (swap a Scrum sprint for a DSDM time-box). The shift: from scientific-management/systems-thinking metaphors to an **ecological** one — manage the environment and see what works.
