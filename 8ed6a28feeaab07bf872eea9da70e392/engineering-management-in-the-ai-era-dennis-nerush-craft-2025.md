# Engineering Management in the AI Era - Dennis Nerush (Talk Outline)

> Dennis Nerush (director of AI engineering, Elementor; ~15 years, 7 in AI) on how AI makes the "**impossible job**" of engineering management better *and* worse — and how to redefine the role.

---

## 1. The Price of Over-Reliance

- Technology makes objects/skills obsolete (address books, paper maps, celestial navigation dropped by the US Navy in 2006) — but over-dependence has a cost (you don't remember phone numbers; can't get back to the hotel without GPS). AI is the new revolution with the same trade-off. The EM role is "impossible" (Michael Lopp): never enough time, outnumbered by "chaotic beautiful snowflakes", too much to know, unattainable expectations.

## 2. Where AI Helps

- **Productivity:** ChatGPT-before-Google, note-takers (tl;dv/Fathom), email summaries (Gemini), presentation drafts (Gamma/Napkin), working prototypes for buy-in (Lovable/Base44/Cursor), and **MCP** connecting Jira/Confluence/Slack to Claude/Cursor (auto-create tickets from a design; an AI companion during incidents pulling logs/postmortems/code).
- **Knowledge consumption:** ChatGPT **scheduled tasks** (a daily leadership-book summary as a push notification), **Deep Research** (26+ sources in ~5.5 min), and **NotebookLM** (turn a report into an *interactive* podcast you can question).
- **Soft skills:** craft measurable performance goals, prep one-on-ones from a Notion database of observations, a custom **"feedback coach" GPT** (teaches a model like SBI, then outlines the conversation — a prep technique, not copy-paste), **voice-mode role-play** of hard conversations, plus hiring (compensating job descriptions, interview questions, candidate scoring, tailored onboarding).
- **Technology:** data-flow/diagram generation from the codebase, **agentic editors** (Cursor/Windsurf) that do the work (with **cursor rules** for standards — naming, structure, tests, PR format, live-updated diagrams), and **Devin** as a first "fully autonomous AI engineer" (a junior teammate in Slack/Jira). Enables EMs to "raise their head" from every PR and be strategic.

## 3. The Dark Side

- Over-reliance has a price: a lawyer lost his license over hallucinated precedents; a **ChatGPT-flavored performance goal** (emoji-laden) deeply offended the recipient — "your people know you're using AI; they want to talk to humans." Agentic editors create **dependency** ("Cursor is down, so we can't work" — but Cursor isn't the developer). AI is great for **experienced** engineers, dangerous for **juniors** who vibe-code without understanding, never boy-scout the codebase (→ mess), and lose their **essence** (critical thinking). The "**expert beginner**" trap accelerates: anyone can build impressive software in minutes and gain **false confidence** that they no longer need to learn best practices — soon we'll have engineers who've never written a line without AI. (The "write tests for this bad function" example: a junior gets 100 tests for code that should have been refactored.)

## 4. The New Role: Balance AI With Being Human

- Beware the dystopian "engineering-manager copilot" that scans all human interaction and auto-sends warnings ("Poopics became passive-aggressive"). Instead: **trust-building**, genuinely knowing your people (families, hobbies, drivers) and connecting their motivation to the company mission, ensuring they **learn and grow** and don't over-depend on AI (use it as a **starting point, not the whole solution**). "Your title makes you a manager; your people make you a leader" (Bill Campbell). Encourage AI adoption *and* remain human-centric. Close (Bruce Lee): "**Be water, my friend**" — adapt to the AI environment while staying focused on what matters: growing your team. Q&A: quality needs **guardrails/definitions** (AI won't magically produce solid production code); NotebookLM won't kill conferences (different medium, like radio/podcasts).
