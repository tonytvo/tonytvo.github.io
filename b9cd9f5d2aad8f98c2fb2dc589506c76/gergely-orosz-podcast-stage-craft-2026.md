# Gergely Orosz – Podcast Stage (Conversation Outline)

> ABK Podcast (Leadership Anonymous) interviews Gergely Orosz at Craft 2026 on engineering leadership in the AI era — a looser, more personal companion to his keynote.

---

## 1. On Writing & Putting Ideas Out

- The *Pragmatic Engineer* blog got no readers for months; the breakout was a Hacker News frontpage (a post arguing code comments signal bad code) that crashed his shared hosting.
- Lesson: **you never know who reads your writing** — keep publishing (blogs, internal emails, sharing with colleagues). Stating the "obvious" often makes others say "me too."
- **AI-written content is detectable and off-putting**: story of a Meta engineer whose good ideas read like "AI slop." Orosz stops reading anything that *feels* like AI — "if it feels like AI, I assume no effort went into it." Advice: write in your own words.

## 2. Human Connection vs. the Illusion of Easy Work

- AI feels easier because it gives the **illusion of replacing hard work** — but output turns generic and less creative.
- A "lighter form of AI psychosis": you assume AI output stays the same quality as before, not noticing gradual degradation (mirrors Meta shipping regressions via trusted AI).
- **Trust doesn't transfer from humans to AI**: with a colleague, trust builds monotonically; AI can hallucinate randomly and silently degrade. Better mental model (via Magda Bernecker / Simon Willison): treat it like a **weird alien**, not a human.
- **Hard work is never wasted** — becoming a pure "AI prompting machine" makes you easily replaceable.

## 3. The Decline of Engineering Management

- Not caused by AI — started with the end of zero-interest-rate money (e.g., Sourcegraph flattening all middle management ~2022–23; Twitter forcing managers to code + carry 20 reports). **AI is accelerating** it.
- Why tech historically had ~6–10 reports/manager (vs. ~40 in other engineering fields): **retention** (a manager who cares reduces attrition) and **managing change** (a good manager stops the team firefighting and spins up a tiger team to fix root causes).
- Prediction: in 2–3 years the industry will **rediscover that good, hands-on, empathetic management matters**.

## 4. Leadership That Works Right Now

- The best-feeling teams have **firm leadership anchored on values, not hype**:
  - opencode's Zach Sward: "we're overusing AI, let's slow down for quality."
  - Linear's CEO publicly refusing layoffs and relentlessly reinforcing quality (300 ms bar).
  - Kelsey Hightower's challenge to startups: **"explain what you do without mentioning AI."**
- Leadership can come from anyone on the team who has the confidence not to just repeat the hype.

## 5. Team Sizes & the Pace of Business

- "Two-pizza teams" (8–10) shrinking toward **one-pizza / 3–4 people** (even John Deere) — but still *teams*.
- Teams thriving with AI (e.g., Linear, ~50 engineers) **kept doing what worked before**: 2–3-person full-stack product-minded teams, demos, intentional shipping — AI just accelerates it somewhat.
- **The pace of business hasn't sped up.** Customers don't want a new product every day; the one place to go fast is **bug fixes**. New-feature velocity beyond every few months yields confusion, not value.
- Industry analogy: teenagers finding an unlocked bar vs. adults who pace themselves.

## 6. "Maybe It's Not That Stupid" — Building the Muscle

- OpenAI ships fast then abandons (Agent Builder → "abandonware"; Sora shut down in 6 months). Looks wasteful — but may build the **muscle of building/launching from scratch**.
- Google's `killedbygoogle` graveyard vs. Amazon's decades-long support: yet Google is the **only** big-tech keeping pace with the labs, because people there "haven't forgotten how to build, launch, and scale." (Apple has no AI strategy; Amazon's Nova flopped; Microsoft Copilot is "an embarrassment.")

## 7. Culture, Morale & Token Economics

- Only **OpenAI and Anthropic** report genuinely happy cultures; elsewhere (even Google's Gemini team, the Copilot team) morale is low despite business success — possibly normal amid unprecedented change.
- **Token maxing is already dead** — killed by usage-based API pricing (Copilot dropped unlimited). The new game is **token *optimizing***: bonuses for saving tokens, smart model routing, and (next) running cheap models like DeepSeek on your own infra. The industry is "speed-running" the cloud/Datadog cost-optimization cycle.
- **IPOs incoming** (Anthropic, SpaceX) may signal a market top; going public means raising the *last* big round from public/retail investors.
- Orosz senses **progress is plateauing** — Opus 4.7/4.8 feel like the first unimpressive/regressing releases (plus over-cautious safety refusals). Even if AI froze today, there's a **decade of value** in building tooling on top of it.

## 8. Advice for (Aspiring) Engineering Managers

- Management is about **people problems** (burnout, sick days, conflict, invisible HR fights) — "managing agents" is not management. The work is fixing broken, invisible things; it's emotionally draining and under-visible.
- For the next ~2 years: **prioritize career stability over people-nurturing** (which won't be valued short-term, then returns).
  - **Get hands-on with AI**; build the **AI infrastructure** your org needs (à la Laura Tacho's system-level maturity) — this makes you highly in-demand.
  - **Cancel half your one-on-ones** and use that time to improve org-level feedback loops.
- Kelsey Hightower's call-center story: instead of working the ticket queue, he **automated the common tickets** (self-service password reset) — ask forgiveness, not permission. Engineers can do the same.
- Closing: **don't stop thinking** — Dax Raad went from "95% thinking / 5% coding" to "96% / 4%" with AI. Thinking and positioning are what win.
