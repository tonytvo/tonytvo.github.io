# Evelyn Van Kelle – Podcast Stage (Conversation Outline)

> ABK / *Leadership Anonymous* interviews **Evelyn van Kelle** (social scientist, change-management consultant, self-described heuristics fan), hosted by Karolina "Fancsi" Tóth. Theme: staying human — intuition, judgment, and behavior design — in an AI-centered world.

---

## 1. AI Won't Replace Human Judgment

- "Will AI replace engineers/managers?" is the wrong, triggering question. **Human judgment is contextual, situational, socially embedded** — reading a room, emotions, conflict, cognitive biases. It's not an either/or; it's about **balance and how one impacts the other**.
- The hidden bad assumption in the question: that **tech is the biggest challenge**. It isn't — the social/human part matters at least as much.
- Response to AI-driven layoffs: **short-sighted**. We won't all become "tech people"; tech people should become **more literate in the human sciences**. Companies will regret it — and indeed CFOs are realizing the fired humans made more money than the AI tokens cost. **Human capital is capital.**

## 2. Frameworks vs. Heuristics

- **Framework** = a prescriptive, well-documented structure you apply (she needles SAFe).
- **Heuristic** = a personal **rule of thumb built from experience**, rarely written down (e.g., "when overwhelmed, go stand outside 5 minutes"; in facilitation, "when I feel we're stuck, step back and observe — is this a me-problem or a group-problem?").
- **The AI risk:** LLM output is mostly *framework-shaped* because it's trained on what was written down — so the **undocumented heuristics get neglected**. When a veteran's gut says "this won't work" but AI says "this will definitely work," we shouldn't blindly trust the framework over hard-won experience.
- Advice: keep a **heuristics journal** per context — heuristics *develop over time* (unlike frozen frameworks), which makes them more valuable. (Host's moving story of clearing her late father's house by remembering "I've done this before" — the essence of a heuristic: trust from past experience.)

## 3. We Aren't Rational — Question Your Own Reasoning

- We pretend we're rational (bring numbers, call it a day) but cognitive-bias research shows we never are.
- We need to **question our own reasoning** more: "did I miss something? are there alternative perspectives? should I talk to someone who disagrees?"
- **AI erases productive disagreement:** it presents confidently, is well-formulated, mirrors your line of thought, and is treated as an **authority** — so we take the easy path and **miss the uncomfortableness of not agreeing**, which is exactly where learning and wisdom live. Preserve **constructive conflict**.

## 4. Responsibility & Ownership

- When an AI suggestion fails, **who's responsible?** Most companies have never even had that discussion; and even where agreements exist, follow-up is weak.
- Don't just **assign** a responsible person or draw a responsibility matrix — "that's just a thing on paper." Because every socio-technical context differs, you must go through **trial, error, and uncomfortable conversations** to learn what works *here*.
- What's needed is a **culture where people feel safe to raise the questions** ("we haven't discussed this") — not a blame culture.

## 5. You Get What You Reinforce

- The central heuristic of the talk: **behavior you want must be reinforced**, or it fades.
- If a team says it values the ability to **override AI suggestions**, then every time someone does, leaders must **visibly value it** (give time to explore alternatives). If instead the response is "that'll take too long, let's just ship the AI answer," the person tries a few more times, then goes quiet — and you lose their wisdom.
- Not disagreement for its own sake — the goal is the capacity for **meaningful disagreement** where you can still agree to disagree.
- Culture (per Marian Hartman) is a **side effect of behaviors** — "how we do things around here." Design it by reinforcing the right behaviors, consistently.

## 6. AI Bias & AI as a New Actor

- AI didn't remove bias — it **amplified** it and gave it a **confident, authoritative writing style**. Biased data in → biased suggestions out → back into our decisions → a **reinforcing bias loop**.
- AI is **not rational** either — it only learns from what we feed it. Troubling research finding: even when humans and AI draw on the same sources, we treat the AI's answer as an **independent additional source**.
- Socio-technical models used to treat the technical side as **passive**; AI is a **new, active actor** on the stage (anthropomorphized — "of course Claude is a man"), which makes the human-relating question far more complex. And it's **not the first disruptive wave** — the recurring questions are the same.

## 7. Engineer the Conditions (Social *and* Technical)

- Organizations obsessively **engineer the technical conditions** (token maxing is a symptom) but neglect **engineering the social dynamics**. We need people who can optimize for **both**.
- There's **no one-size-fits-all**: "deliberate thinking" means something different in every team, so it can't be blueprinted — it takes time, dedication, and social-focused people. The blocker is usually time/money/**willingness**.

## 8. Advice for Leaders

- **You can't change people, but you can change the environment.** Leaders should ask "what do we value?" then do **critical self-reflection: is that what we're actually reinforcing?** Close the gap between what you want and how you act.
- Example: teams say they value deliberate thinking, but reinforce **speed** — that misalignment is the leader's responsibility to fix.
- A leader is an **anchor**: your behavior signals "how we do things here." Watch not only what you reinforce in others but **what is being reinforced in you**.
- New power-dynamics question (not a tech question): **when the senior engineer and the AI disagree, who wins?** AI-as-authority reshapes organizational power.
- Closing: get **curious and honest with yourself**, go into the fear, and **keep talking to people** — not just the AI. Seek community and, if needed, expert facilitation.
