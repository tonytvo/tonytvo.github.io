# Mind Your P's and Queues - Daniel Vacanti (Talk Outline)

> Daniel Vacanti ("mind your P's and Q's" = *watch what you're doing*) on the agile obsession with **P**rioritization versus the neglected thing that actually determines outcomes: your **Q**ueues (work in progress).

---

## 1. The Point of Prioritization

- Agile frameworks mandate prioritization (Scrum's ordered backlog, SAFe's WSJF) to **maximize value**. But value is only realized when an item is **delivered to the customer** — so what we truly care about is the **order in which items *finish***. Prioritization only sets the **order in which items *start***. It assumes items that start sooner finish sooner and finish roughly in start order — which almost nobody verifies.

## 2. Queues Are Everywhere

- What influences *when items finish* is **how many things you're working on at once** (queue size / WIP). Boards, work-breakdown levels (initiative → feature → team) are all queues. Pulling one **story** from feature A/B/C/D/E quietly pulls **five features** into progress — an **explosion of WIP** across the org that looks disciplined at the story level.

## 3. Monte Carlo & the WIP Effect

- Customers only ask "**when will it be done / how many features make the date?**" Monte Carlo simulation can answer it — but assumes one-thing-at-a-time and strict priority order (never true). Modeling higher WIP shows priorities **scramble**: at WIP=9, priority #1 has a *lower* chance of finishing than #2–#15; pull everything into progress and "stories not assigned to a feature" beat "bank setup" (priority #1). As WIP grows, **finish order is dictated by WIP, not priority** — so all that prioritization effort is wasted. (SAFe's WSJF implementation gets extra criticism.)

## 4. Takeaway & Q&A

- If priority is meaningless, why plan? If you can't plan, you can't be predictable; if you can't be predictable, you can't decide where to invest. Every agile "P" comes back to **controlling queue size**.
- Q&A: *not* "work on one thing only" — find your **optimal capacity** (probably not 18). **No ideal team size** (he ran a 60-person standup under 15 min) — the limit is *how many things* you work on, not headcount. **Lean + agile** belong together ("no agile without lean"). Book path: *When Will It Be Done?* → *Actionable Agile Metrics* → the third. Queuing theory: read John Little; variation: Shewhart/Deming/Wheeler.
