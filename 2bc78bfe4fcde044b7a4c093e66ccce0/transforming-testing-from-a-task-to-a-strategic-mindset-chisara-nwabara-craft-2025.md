# Transforming Testing from a Task to a Strategic Mindset - Chisara Nwabara (Talk Outline)

> Chisara Nwabara on why delivery projects fail — applying a testing/experiment mindset far beyond the Jira backlog to define success and create collaboration across the product life cycle.

---

## 1. A Testing Mindset (Confessions)

- Do only what's necessary: **test only what you need now**, big rocks first. **Don't repeat yourself** — establish patterns/automation/presets. The only real failure is doing something and **not learning** whether it was a good or bad idea (a win either way). Save time by planning enough up front to **test the plan well**.

## 2. Defining Success — Beyond the Backlog

- Reuse the familiar trio, but apply it everywhere. **Definition of ready** — ensure readiness before starting (a checklist; be honest about what you do/don't have — e.g. a **shared project name**, and clear **scoping evidence**). **Acceptance criteria** — capture assumptions and expected **behaviors/outcomes** before building (given/when/then), tied back to value, not just tasks. **Definition of done** — how do you know it's done, across **tangibles** (code committed, tests passing/recorded, docs published, features deployed) and **intangibles** (feature confidence, stakeholder approval, **team health**, release readiness). Apply the same "before / during / after" thinking to project plans, OKRs, and go-to-market plans.

## 3. Six Common Pitfalls

- **1. Jumping to solutions too quickly** — you move fast in the wrong direction. Start with **problems** (Einstein: 99% on the problem), capture context/cause/consequence simply, then a **hypothesis** ("by doing X for these people we'll achieve Y, known by this market feedback"). Don't skip discovery. (Example: couldn't forecast a procedures project until she mapped **~15 procedures** by low/medium/high complexity.)
- **2. Doers have no idea why** — if you can't draw a line from company vision down to the smallest subtask, you may be wasting time. Use **taxonomy** (like the animal kingdom) so people see how work fits; a clean backlog answers "why is this taking so long?"
- **3. A brain dump is not a project plan** — clarity is key (clear up close, blurrier further out, but you can see the road). Maintain a **single source of truth** (reliable, accessible, timely). Paper trails cut onboarding from **two months to 20 minutes**.
- **4. Assuming everyone understands the assignment** — collaborate early, align often; identify stakeholders early (a **release train** built *with* the stakeholders instead of giving them 1–2 days' notice). "Leave no one behind."
- **5. You're doing too much** — too many things at once invalidates the experiment (can't isolate variables) and yields poor quality ("what goes in must come out"). Mitigate with partial implementations, riskiest-assumption-first, feature flags, prototypes/pilots/**phased releases** (like transitioning a dog's food gradually). Do **real capacity planning** for actual humans.
- **6. Fumbling order of operations** — writing the test after the exam. **TDD isn't just for coding**: state the question/problem, your believed answer, attempt to solve, learn from the variance. Write it down or scope will creep ("those who stand for nothing fall for everything").

## 4. Takeaways

- Start with an opportunity/problem and a hypothesis; ensure clarity and alignment; prioritize and iterate; follow the correct order of operations. **Be honest** about how much accountability you actually have (everyone is responsible for quality). **Test your riskiest assumptions first**, seek significant results (a clear yes or no beats "I don't know"), and **plan first, then act**. The leaving question: *how might we make it safe for people to do the right thing?*
