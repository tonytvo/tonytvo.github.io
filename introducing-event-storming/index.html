Silos: How Organizations Naturally Evolve into Silos and Their Impact on Efficiency

Silos are a natural consequence of organizational growth. As a company expands, roles become specialized, leading to barriers to effective communication and collaboration. The author describes this as organizational gravity, meaning that businesses tend to fall into similar dysfunctional patterns regardless of their industry or structure.

‚ÄúSilos will emerge by default in any organization. They‚Äôre easy to establish and very hard to remove.‚Äù

How Silos Form

The formation of silos follows a predictable pattern:

  1. Someone becomes overwhelmed managing multiple tasks.
  2. They hire a specialist to take over a specific function.
  3. The new hire starts contributing but remains focused only on their area, developing expertise in isolation.
  4. The former expert loses touch with the details of that area.
  5. The cycle repeats, with more roles being specialized, leading to knowledge fragmentation.

The unintended consequence is that employees optimize for their own department rather than the company as a whole, leading to local optimizations but global inefficiencies.

‚ÄúIn the long term, silos maximize ignorance about the whole.‚Äù

Silos also result in a lack of cross-functional learning. The problem is not just inefficiency but that knowledge does not transfer, making problem-solving more difficult across teams.

Targets and Goals: The Issue with Management-Driven Goal-Setting Processes

The way organizations set targets and goals often creates hidden conflicts and inefficiencies. While goal-setting seems logical, secrecy and misalignment in corporate objectives can cause serious organizational dysfunction.

‚ÄúIndividual goals and targets and the possible monetary rewards that come with them are not discussed publicly. But this means there is no safety check that the goals are compatible between themselves.‚Äù

The Problem with Traditional Goal-Setting

  1. Secrecy in targets ‚Äì Managers often privately agree on their goals, which may conflict with the goals of other teams.
  2. Conflicting priorities ‚Äì Different departments compete for limited resources instead of collaborating.
  3. Bonuses create perverse incentives ‚Äì Employees may game the system to meet targets at the expense of the broader organization.
  4. Unrealistic expectations ‚Äì Management often demands results that exceed team capacity, leading to stress and inefficiency.

The book illustrates this problem with an example:

‚ÄúEvery manager thought they had rights to a 40% of the pie. But there were seven managers, and the pie was not big enough for all of them.‚Äù

This scenario describes how stakeholders refuse to compromise on deadlines because their bonuses depend on them, even when deadlines are clearly unachievable.

Decisions‚Äô Lifecycle: Understanding How Decisions Evolve Within a Business

‚ÄúDecisions will pile up.‚Äù

Decisions rarely get revisited or undone, leading to a growing list of outdated, ineffective policies. Organizations often keep old decisions in place long after they are useful, creating unnecessary complexity.

Why Old Decisions Persist

  1. Fear of admitting mistakes ‚Äì Decision-makers see reversing a choice as an admission of failure.
  2. Bias toward adding, not removing ‚Äì It‚Äôs easier to keep adding new rules than to simplify existing ones.
  3. Organizational inertia ‚Äì Once a policy is in place, no one wants to be responsible for removing it.

An example from the book:

‚ÄúPeople in different departments had the illusion of collaborating because they performed useless duties for their colleagues.‚Äù

This illustrates how old decisions remain unchallenged, even when they no longer provide value.

The Cost of Agreeing: Challenges of Achieving Alignment in Complex Systems

‚ÄúCentralized, top-down decisions still have the advantage of speed of execution. In contrast, collaborative decisions require reaching an agreement, and achieving it could be incredibly demanding in terms of time, energy, and coordination.‚Äù

While collaborative decision-making is ideal, it is often slow and difficult to implement. Top-down decisions move faster, but they can be misaligned with the realities of teams executing them.

Key points:

  1. Alignment takes time ‚Äì The more people involved, the harder it is to reach a consensus.
  2. Revisiting past decisions is hard ‚Äì Decisions become part of corporate identity, making change difficult.
  3. Fear of challenging leadership ‚Äì Employees may avoid questioning bad decisions because it could be seen as disrespecting authority.

The book references Architectural Decision Records (ADRs) as a way to document decisions along with their original context, making it easier to evaluate when a decision should be reversed.

  • Second-hand learning is inefficient‚Äîmisinterpretations multiply at each layer.
  • A single person (PO) becomes a bottleneck, limiting knowledge flow.

  2. Iterations reinforce existing biases, making it harder to step back and rethink fundamental assumptions.

‚ÄúSome projects follow the plan relatively well‚Äîcompliance projects, for example. But if your project involves innovation, you need to make room for discovery, not just execution.‚Äù

Rather than following a rigid backlog, EventStorming enables teams to uncover what‚Äôs truly important through real-time collaboration and dynamic modeling.

Emerging Structure

‚ÄúA chaotic collection of events is only the beginning‚Äîstructure must emerge.‚Äù

EventStorming starts as an exploration of events, but as patterns appear, structure emerges naturally. This structure helps teams organize workflows, find dependencies, and improve system understanding.

4.1. Key Events

‚ÄúKey events signal a pivotal change in the narrative.‚Äù

Key events are significant moments in a business process, often marking shifts in responsibility or state. For example:

  • In sales, a key event might be ‚ÄúContract Signed‚Äù, shifting responsibility from Sales to Fulfillment.
  • In e-commerce, a key event could be ‚ÄúPayment Processed‚Äù, leading to order fulfillment.

‚ÄúWe also look for highly duplicated events or marked changes in language as clues that indicate key events.‚Äù

Key events serve as structural anchors in the EventStorming timeline. Teams use them as boundaries to break processes into meaningful chunks.

4.2. Milestones

‚ÄúMilestones drive the process forward‚Äîthey are deadlines, not events.‚Äù

Milestones mark date-driven deadlines rather than system events. For example:

  • A product launch may have milestones like ‚ÄúBeta Released,‚Äù ‚ÄúMarketing Campaign Launched,‚Äù ‚ÄúPublic Release.‚Äù
  • A conference has milestones for speaker confirmations, venue booking, and ticket sales deadlines.

‚ÄúThe most interesting learning in the conversation about a milestone is typically triggered when you ask, ‚ÄòWhat happens if this milestone is missed?‚Äô‚Äù

Teams should explore failure scenarios around milestones. Missed deadlines often reveal hidden bottlenecks.

4.3. Alternate Paths

‚ÄúNot all processes follow a single happy path‚Äîalternate paths must be explored.‚Äù

To represent different experiences and subprocesses, swimlanes help differentiate:

  • Automated vs. manual workflows (e.g., ‚ÄúOrder Approved Automatically‚Äù vs. ‚ÄúManual Review Required‚Äù).
  • Success vs. failure outcomes (e.g., ‚ÄúPayment Processed‚Äù vs. ‚ÄúPayment Failed‚Äù).

‚ÄúSwimlanes make it easier to visualize dependencies and handoffs.‚Äù

By mapping alternate flows, teams proactively identify failure points and inefficiencies.

4.4. Themes

‚ÄúSome patterns appear repeatedly across the timeline‚Äîthese should be treated as themes.‚Äù

A theme is a set of related events that cut across different parts of the process, such as:

  • Security ‚Äì Login, authentication, authorization checks.
  • Monitoring ‚Äì Logging, tracking, error handling.
  • Compliance ‚Äì Data retention, auditing, approvals.

‚ÄúThemes should be extracted to reduce clutter and improve focus on the primary workflow.‚Äù

4.5. Facilitation Tips

‚ÄúA facilitator‚Äôs role shifts from generating discussion to guiding structure.‚Äù

Facilitators should:

  • Use colors or lines to differentiate structural elements like swimlanes, milestones, and themes.

‚ÄúA poor domain model slows down the business. A well-defined set of bounded contexts accelerates innovation.‚Äù

  3. They capture meaningful change ‚Äì A Domain Event always represents a state transition in a system.
  4. They drive reactive systems ‚Äì Events trigger subsequent actions, forming the backbone of event-driven architectures.

‚ÄúIf no one cares about an event, maybe it‚Äôs not that relevant in your system.‚Äù

Unlike database records, Domain Events focus on the business narrative‚Äîthey tell a story of what happened rather than just storing data.

Events as State Transitions

‚ÄúUsing a verb in past tense forces us to explore the exact moment when something changes.‚Äù

Domain Events mark critical state transitions in a system. These transitions:

  • Define key moments in a process (e.g., Order Placed, Payment Processed, Order Shipped).
  • Clarify dependencies (e.g., an Order cannot be shipped before Payment is processed).
  • Create opportunities for automation (e.g., a Shipped event triggers an email notification).

‚ÄúA Domain Event is not just a log entry‚Äîit represents a meaningful change that the system must react to.‚Äù

Example: Modeling State Transitions in Temperature Monitoring
  1. Temperature Raised ‚Äì An imprecise event; it lacks clarity.
  2. Temperature Registered ‚Äì More precise, capturing what happened and when.
  3. Temperature Threshold Exceeded ‚Äì A state transition that may trigger an alert.

‚ÄúDon‚Äôt try to make events too precise too early‚Äîembrace fuzziness until patterns emerge.‚Äù

By iterating on event definitions, teams gain a deeper understanding of business processes.

Commands, Actions, and Decisions

‚ÄúCommands are user or system actions that trigger events‚Äîbut not every command leads to a successful event.‚Äù

Distinguishing Between Commands, Actions, and Decisions

  1. Commands (Blue Stickies) ‚Äì A request for an action (e.g., Place Order, Submit Payment).
  2. Actions ‚Äì The execution of a command, but not all actions result in events (e.g., A payment attempt might fail).
  3. Decisions ‚Äì A business rule that determines an outcome (e.g., Fraud Check Approved leads to Order Processed).

‚ÄúA command does not imply completion‚Äîevents will eventually reflect its outcome.‚Äù

Example: Ordering a Pizza Online

  • Command: Place Order
  • Decision: Verify Payment (Approved or Denied)
  • Event: Order Placed (if successful)

‚ÄúIf something fails, an event should still be recorded‚Äîfailure is part of the system‚Äôs story too.‚Äù

By separating commands from events, teams build resilient, event-driven systems where failure is explicitly handled.

Key Takeaways

  • Unlike Big Picture workshops, Design-Level EventStorming is about converging, not diverging.
  • Events come from multiple sources‚Äîunderstanding them ensures realistic modeling.
  • Aggregates should be discovered by analyzing responsibilities, not by forcing a predefined structure.
  • The workshop is done when all major design concerns are addressed, not when everything is ‚Äòperfect.‚Äô.

‚ÄúEventStorming helps teams design software in a way that aligns with business reality‚Äînot just technical constraints.‚Äù

By visualizing alternatives, delaying premature decisions, focusing on the right problems, and refining models incrementally, teams create better, more maintainable software architectures.

By running a Design-Level EventStorming workshop, teams ensure their software models truly reflect business realities‚Äîleading to better architecture, maintainability, and scalability.

‚ÄúEventStorming helps teams shift from CRUD-based thinking to event-driven modeling‚Äîfocusing on what truly matters: meaningful change.‚Äù

By using Domain Events, State Transitions, and Commands effectively, teams can model business processes that are scalable, reactive, and aligned with real-world behaviors.
