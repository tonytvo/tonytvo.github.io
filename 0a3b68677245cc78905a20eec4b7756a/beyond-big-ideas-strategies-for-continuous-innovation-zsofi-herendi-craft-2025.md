# Beyond Big Ideas: Strategies for Continuous Innovation - Zsófi Herendi (Workshop Outline)

> Zsófi Herendi (product manager at Tesco Technology, visual thinker, DDD Hungary founder) runs a hands-on session combining the **Business Model Canvas** with ideation techniques to build shared understanding and unlock continuous innovation.

---

## 1. The Problem: False Agreement

- After 20+ years bridging business and developers, the recurring pain was being unable to answer engineers' *why* questions about roadmap items (what problem, which users, why this?) — priorities came down from leadership as "aligned with strategic goals" with no detail. The biggest risk isn't disagreement, it's **false agreement**: thinking you understand each other when you don't. A visual tool — the **Business Model Canvas** — helps verbalize the "obvious," giving just enough structure to think together and act as a launchpad for innovation. **"Never write a line of code without knowing why."**

## 2. Business Model Canvas Basics

- Business models are how an organization **creates, delivers, and captures value**. The canvas (Alex Osterwalder, 2008) maps any business in **nine sections**, filled in a story-telling order: **Customer segments** (whom you serve — first and most important), **value propositions**, **channels**, **customer relationships**, **revenue streams**, then the internal blue side: **key activities**, **key resources**, **key partners**, and **cost structure** (Netflix used as the worked example). Osterwalder's own learning: it "goes beyond the blah blah blah" — forcing real shared understanding.
- Use the **Lean Startup Canvas** for brand-new ideas from scratch; the **Business Model Canvas** for existing businesses. Herendi mixes in the missing pieces she values: **problem statement**, **unfair/competitive advantage**, **existing alternatives** (how customers cope without you), **early adopters**, **metrics** (how you know you're successful — the one she missed most), and **high-level concept**.

## 3. Four Levels & the Core Takeaway

- The levels: **checklist** (fill in the fields), **story** (tell how the pieces connect in order), **patterns** (study others' business-model dynamics), and **evolution** (experiment with different models). The single takeaway: **never start anything without visualizing and understanding the context** — gain situational awareness (echoing Simon Wardley) so you don't "build blind."

## 4. Innovation as a Process

- ~20% of failed startups die from a **wrong business model**. Companies that treated crises (e.g. the pandemic) as accelerators had **exploration processes** already in place — running experiments alongside exploiting today's model (no "Chinese wall" between exploit and explore). Innovation isn't a lone genius idea; it's **processes and tools** plus curiosity. Leadership shouldn't hand-pick "the winning idea" (a project mindset of deadlines and success/failure) but let many ideas evolve.
- **Where to innovate** (from *The Invincible Company*, summarized into nine "disruption cards"): don't only tweak the value proposition (new feature / lower price isn't real disruption). **Channel kings**, **market explorers** (Tesla — new segments), **gravity creators** (hard to switch — Windows, Meetup), **resource castles**, **activity differentiators**, **scalers** (IKEA turning customers into assemblers), and **revenue/cost/margin differentiators** (**Nespresso** — recurring pod sales + selling pods only in its own stores; its 1987 office-focused model *failed* on the same technology until the business model changed).
- **Exercises:** map a chosen business on the canvas; **escape thinking** (remove one attribute and imagine how the business works without it); and step into an innovator's shoes using the disruption cards. Look for **alternatives, not correct answers** — free the mind, and don't stop just because an idea seems crazy. Walk-away: visualize and deeply understand the context, keep curiosity alive, and remember **innovation is about processes and tools**.
